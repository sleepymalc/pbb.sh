**TL;DR.** Scaling influence-function methods to large models means compressing per-example gradients with random projection, and the textbook justification is Johnson–Lindenstrauss. But JL preserves Euclidean distances, while influence relies on *inverting* a curvature matrix — a fundamentally different problem. We replace the JL argument with a tight theory keyed to the curvature's **effective dimension** at the regularization scale — an instance-adaptive quantity that can be far smaller than the rank — with matching upper and lower bounds across unregularized, ridge-regularized, and Kronecker-factored settings, plus a leakage term for test gradients that fall outside the curvature's support.

Influence functions and most modern data-attribution scores share the form $\tau_\lambda(g, g') = g^\top (F + \lambda I)^{-1} g'$, where $F \succeq 0$ is a curvature operator (Hessian, GGN, empirical Fisher). At neural-network scale, $F \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is unformable and uninvertible, so practitioners sketch with $P \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d}$ and replace the score by $\tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g') = (Pg)^\top (PFP^\top + \lambda I_m)^{-1}(Pg')$. The standard justification — "by Johnson–Lindenstrauss" — is a category error: JL preserves Euclidean distances, but $\tau_\lambda$ is sensitive to *inversion*, not to $\lVert Pg \rVert_2$. We replace that folk argument with a precise, instance-adaptive theory of when projection provably preserves influence.

## Why this theory

- **JL is the wrong lemma.** Sketching changes the operator that gets inverted; preserving $\lVert Pg - Pg' \rVert_2$ says nothing about $(PFP^\top + \lambda I)^{-1}$. Empirically, projected-influence quality is *not* monotone in $m$ — exactly the symptom of an inverse-sensitive object analyzed with a Euclidean tool.
- **Effective dimension is the right quantity.** Without regularization, exact preservation forces $m \geq \mathrm{rank}(F)$ — typically $\Theta(n)$. With ridge $\lambda > 0$, the required sketch size collapses to $d_\lambda(F) = \sum_j \lambda_j(F)/(\lambda_j(F) + \lambda)$, which for fast-decaying spectra is $\ll \mathrm{rank}(F) \ll d$. Upper and lower bounds match.
- **KFAC has a quantifiable statistical cost.** A factorized sketch $P = P_A \otimes P_E$ saves compute, but pays $\varepsilon^{-4}$ instead of $\varepsilon^{-2}$: $m_A m_E = \tilde{\Omega}(\varepsilon^{-4} \cdot d_{\lambda_E}(A)\, d_{\lambda_A}(E))$. The first explicit computational–statistical tradeoff for Kronecker sketches.
- **Out-of-range test gradients leak — but predictably.** When $g'$ has a component $g'_\perp \in \ker(F)$, the *true* score is unaffected, but the *sketched* score acquires a leakage term we bound explicitly in $\lambda$ and $\lVert F \rVert_2$.
- **Operational guidance.** A two-stage recipe falls out: pick $\lambda^\star$ for utility on a small validation set, then scale $m$ to roughly $C \cdot d_{\lambda^\star}(F)$ with a modest $C \in (10, 100)$.

## Method

### Setup: a bilinear inverse-sensitive functional

With ridge parameter $\lambda \geq 0$, the (true) regularized influence and its sketched counterpart are

$$
\tau_\lambda(g, g') \coloneqq g^\top (F + \lambda I_d)^{-1} g',
\qquad
\tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g') \coloneqq (Pg)^\top (PFP^\top + \lambda I_m)^{-1} (Pg').
$$

Let $r \coloneqq \mathrm{rank}(F)$ with compact eigendecomposition $F = U \Lambda U^\top$ ($U \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ orthonormal, $\Lambda \succ 0$). We take $P$ to be an *oblivious sketching matrix*: rows $P_i^\top = \frac{1}{\sqrt{m}} W_i^\top$ with $W_i$ i.i.d.\ isotropic sub-Gaussian — covering Gaussian, Rademacher, and sparse JL transforms.

### Unregularized regime: a sharp phase transition

With $\lambda = 0$, the resolvent $F^{-1}$ has no damping and any collapse on $\mathrm{range}(F)$ is fatal.

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Unregularized barrier). *For $g, g' \in \mathrm{range}(F)$, $\tilde\tau_0 = \tau_0$ for all such $g, g'$ if and only if $P$ is injective on $\mathrm{range}(F)$, i.e., $\mathrm{rank}(PU) = r$. If $\mathrm{rank}(PU) < r$ — in particular if $m < r$ — no uniform multiplicative approximation is possible over $\mathrm{range}(F) \setminus \{0\}$, at any constant factor.*

</div>

Either the sketch is injective on $\mathrm{range}(F)$ (and influence is preserved *exactly* with probability one for continuous sketches), or no $\varepsilon$ guarantee exists at all. In overparametrized regimes $r \approx n$, so $m$ must scale with the dataset.

### Regularized regime: effective dimension is tight

Ridge regularization changes the geometry: directions with $\lambda_j(F) \ll \lambda$ are already dominated by $\lambda$ in $F + \lambda I$, so they need not be preserved multiplicatively. The binding quantity is the **effective dimension**

$$
d_\lambda(F) \;\coloneqq\; \operatorname{tr}\!\bigl(F (F + \lambda I)^{-1}\bigr) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{r} \frac{\lambda_j(F)}{\lambda_j(F) + \lambda} \;\leq\; r,
$$

a classical Bayesian model-selection quantity that counts only eigenvalues large relative to $\lambda$.

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Regularized upper bound). *For oblivious $P$, $\lambda > 0$, and any $\varepsilon, \delta \in (0, 1)$, if*

$$
m \;=\; \Omega\!\left( \frac{d_\lambda(F) + \log(1/\delta)}{\varepsilon^2} \right),
$$

*then with probability at least $1 - \delta$, for all $g, g' \in \mathrm{range}(F)$,*

$$
\bigl\lvert \tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g') - \tau_\lambda(g, g') \bigr\rvert \;\leq\; \varepsilon \sqrt{\tau_0(g, g)}\,\sqrt{\tau_0(g', g')}.
$$

</div>

The proof hinges on the *$\lambda$-whitened influence subspace*

$$
B \coloneqq F^{1/2} (F + \lambda I)^{-1/2},
\qquad B B^\top = B^\top B = F (F + \lambda I)^{-1},
$$

which captures the directions that actually need to survive the sketch: eigendirections of $F$ damped by $\lambda$ contribute weight $\lambda_j(F)/(\lambda_j(F)+\lambda)$ to $BB^\top$, recovering $d_\lambda(F) = \operatorname{tr}(BB^\top)$. The argument reduces to controlling $\lVert B^\top (P^\top P - I) B \rVert_2 \leq \varepsilon/2$, which yields a **ridge-regularized sandwich** $(1 - \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2})(F + \lambda I) \preceq G + \lambda I \preceq (1 + \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2})(F + \lambda I)$ for $G \coloneqq F^{1/2} P^\top P F^{1/2}$, and then resolvent perturbation through $A(A + \lambda I)^{-1} = I - \lambda(A + \lambda I)^{-1}$. The key relaxation over an oblivious subspace embedding (which would force $m = \Omega(r/\varepsilon^2)$ via uniform multiplicative accuracy on $\mathrm{range}(F^{1/2})$) is that we only need approximate isometry on the *whitened* subspace $B$, not on $F^{1/2}$ itself.

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Lower bound). *For Gaussian oblivious $P$, there exists a family of $F \succeq 0$ such that if $m = o(d_\lambda(F)/\varepsilon^2)$, then some $g \in \mathrm{range}(F)$ admits $\lvert \tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g) - \tau_\lambda(g, g) \rvert = \Omega(\varepsilon)\,\tau_0(g, g)$ with constant probability.*

</div>

The construction takes a diagonal $F$ with a top block of $k$ eigenvalues at scale $\lambda$ — so $d_\lambda(F) = \Theta(k)$ — and reduces sketched influence to an anti-concentration bound on the Wishart matrix $\tfrac{1}{m} W_L^\top W_L$. Upper and lower bounds match: $d_\lambda(F)$ is the *fundamental* complexity parameter.

### Factorized curvature: KFAC pays $\varepsilon^{-4}$

K-FAC approximates each layer's curvature as $F \approx A \otimes E$, with $A \in \mathbb{R}^{d_A \times d_A}$ the activation covariance and $E \in \mathbb{R}^{d_E \times d_E}$ the backprop-gradient covariance. The natural sketch is also factorized, $P = P_A \otimes P_E$, so that $Pg = \operatorname{vec}(P_E G P_A^\top)$ and $PFP^\top = (P_A A P_A^\top) \otimes (P_E E P_E^\top)$ can be formed without materializing $P$. But Kronecker rows are correlated, so the i.i.d.-row concentration that drives the unfactorized proof breaks; we replace it with a dedicated *factorized covariance deviation bound* on $\lVert B^\top (P^\top P - I) B \rVert_2$.

The unregularized barrier becomes stricter: $\tilde\tau_0 = \tau_0$ on $\mathrm{range}(F)$ iff $P_A$ is injective on $\mathrm{range}(A)$ *and* $P_E$ is injective on $\mathrm{range}(E)$, forcing $m_A \geq \mathrm{rank}(A)$, $m_E \geq \mathrm{rank}(E)$, and hence $m = m_A m_E \geq \mathrm{rank}(F)$.

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Factorized regularized upper bound). *Let $F = A \otimes E$, $P = P_A \otimes P_E$, $\lambda \leq \lVert A \rVert_2 \lVert E \rVert_2$, and define $\lambda_E \coloneqq \lambda/\lVert E \rVert_2$, $\lambda_A \coloneqq \lambda/\lVert A \rVert_2$. If*

$$
m_A \,=\, \Omega\!\left(\frac{d_{\lambda_E}(A) + \log(1/\delta)}{\varepsilon^2}\right),
\qquad
m_E \,=\, \Omega\!\left(\frac{d_{\lambda_A}(E) + \log(1/\delta)}{\varepsilon^2}\right),
$$

*then with probability $\geq 1 - \delta$, $\lvert \tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g') - \tau_\lambda(g, g') \rvert \leq \varepsilon \sqrt{\tau_0(g, g)} \sqrt{\tau_0(g', g')}$ for all $g, g' \in \mathrm{range}(F)$.*

</div>

Two consequences. First, each mode is regularized by the *opposing* factor's signal strength: $A$ sees ridge $\lambda_E = \lambda/\lVert E \rVert_2$, and vice versa. Second, the *total* sketch size is

$$
m \,=\, m_A m_E \,=\, \tilde\Omega\!\left( \varepsilon^{-4} \cdot d_{\lambda_E}(A)\, d_{\lambda_A}(E) \right),
$$

a strict $\varepsilon^{-2} \to \varepsilon^{-4}$ degradation over the unfactorized bound — a price paid for separability, not an artifact of loose analysis.

### Out-of-range leakage

For an unseen test gradient $g'$, decompose $g' = g'_\parallel + g'_\perp$ with $g'_\parallel \in \mathrm{range}(F)$ and $g'_\perp \in \ker(F)$. The *true* influence ignores $g'_\perp$: $\tau_\lambda(g, g'_\perp) = 0$. The *sketched* influence can pick up a sketch-induced leakage $\tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g'_\perp)$ because $P^\top P$ couples $\mathrm{range}(F)$ and $\ker(F)$. The leakage decomposes cleanly:

$$
\bigl\lvert \tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g') - \tau_\lambda(g, g') \bigr\rvert \;\leq\; \underbrace{\bigl\lvert \tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g'_\parallel) - \tau_\lambda(g, g'_\parallel) \bigr\rvert}_{\text{Theorem above}} \;+\; \underbrace{\bigl\lvert \tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g'_\perp) \bigr\rvert}_{\text{leakage}}.
$$

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Leakage). *For $k$ test gradients with $k' \coloneqq \dim \mathrm{Span}\{g'_{j,\perp}\}_{j=1}^k$, if $m = \Omega((r + \min\{\log(k/\delta),\, k' + \log(1/\delta)\})/\varepsilon^2)$, then with probability $\geq 1 - \delta$, uniformly in $j$,*

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Unreg.:} \quad &\bigl\lvert \tilde\tau_0(g, g'_{j,\perp}) \bigr\rvert \;\leq\; \varepsilon \,\lVert g \rVert_2 \,\lVert g'_{j,\perp} \rVert_2 \,/\, \lambda_{\min}^+(F), \\
\text{Reg.:} \quad &\bigl\lvert \tilde\tau_\lambda(g, g'_{j,\perp}) \bigr\rvert \;\leq\; \varepsilon \,\lVert g \rVert_2 \,\lVert g'_{j,\perp} \rVert_2 \!\left( \frac{1}{\lambda} + \frac{2 \lVert F \rVert_2}{\lambda^2} \right).
\end{aligned}
$$

</div>

Both bounds decay at the standard $O(m^{-1/2})$ rate; the regularized bound is explicit in $\lambda$ and $\lVert F \rVert_2$, formalizing the cost of operating off the curvature's support. An analogous factorized statement (Kronecker $P = P_A \otimes P_E$) follows via a factor-level cross-term lemma, with the same primitives controlled by union-bound or subspace arguments.

## Results

### Sketch-size guidance, by regime

The three regimes give a clean decision tree for choosing $m$:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Regime | Curvature | Sketch | Sketch size $m$ | Comment |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Unregularized** | $F$, $\lambda = 0$ | oblivious | $m \geq \mathrm{rank}(F) \approx n$ | Phase transition: exact or no guarantee. |
| **Regularized** | $F + \lambda I$, $\lambda > 0$ | oblivious | $\Omega((d_\lambda(F) + \log(1/\delta))/\varepsilon^2)$ | Tight: matching $\Omega$ lower bound for Gaussian $P$. |
| **Factorized** | $F = A \otimes E$, $\lambda > 0$ | $P_A \otimes P_E$ | $\tilde\Omega(\varepsilon^{-4} \cdot d_{\lambda_E}(A)\, d_{\lambda_A}(E))$ | Computational–statistical tradeoff: $\varepsilon^{-2} \to \varepsilon^{-4}$. |
| **Out-of-range** | any of the above | oblivious or factorized | extra $\log(k)$ or $k'$ term | Leakage scales as $\varepsilon(1/\lambda + 2\lVert F \rVert_2/\lambda^2)$. |

</div>

Because $d_\lambda(F)$ is *instance-adaptive*, the guidance is automatically tighter than worst-case rank bounds whenever the Fisher spectrum decays. On the experiments in the paper (MNIST + LR/MLP, CIFAR-2 + ResNet9 with empirical Fisher), the eigenvalues of $F$ decay sharply enough that $d_\lambda(F) \ll r \ll d$ for moderate $\lambda$, and the predicted scaling $m \sim d_\lambda(F)$ matches the observed normalized error curves with a modest hidden constant.

### What JL gets wrong

Plotted on the same axes, the JL bound and our bound diverge in both directions:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Setting | JL prediction ($m = O(\log n/\varepsilon^2)$) | Our theory |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Unregularized, $r \approx n$ | $m \sim \log n$ | $m \geq r$ — **JL underestimates by a factor of $n/\log n$**. |
| Regularized, fast-decaying spectrum | $m \sim \log n$ | $m \sim d_\lambda(F)$, often $\ll \log n$ for tight $\lambda$ — **JL can overestimate**. |
| Factorized | $m \sim \log n$ (no $\varepsilon$ dependence stated) | $\tilde\Omega(\varepsilon^{-4} d_{\lambda_E}(A) d_{\lambda_A}(E))$ — **JL silent on KFAC structure**. |

</div>

The point isn't that JL is wrong about distances; it's that influence is *not a distance*. The right concentration object is $\lVert B^\top(P^\top P - I) B \rVert_2$ on the $\lambda$-whitened subspace, and once that is in hand the three regimes fall out as instances of one resolvent perturbation argument.

### Experiments

We empirically test the theory on three dattri-style settings — MNIST + LR, MNIST + MLP, and CIFAR-2 + ResNet9 — each with $5000$ training and $500$ test examples (so $r \leq 5000$), the empirical Fisher as $F$, and a sparse JL transform as $P$.

#### Faithfulness ≠ utility: the best $\lambda^\star$ is intermediate

A small $\varepsilon_\lambda$ does not imply a useful score: making $\lambda$ very large makes the inverse less sensitive to sketching (so $\varepsilon_\lambda$ shrinks) but also washes out the curvature signal in $F$. We see this directly in [LDS](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.00622):

<figure class="figure-block">
  <img src="/research/Projection-IF/Figures/faithfulness_utility_combined.png" alt="Top row: LDS versus lambda on the three settings, with different colors for different sketch sizes m. Each curve peaks at an intermediate lambda. Bottom row: 95th-percentile approximation error versus lambda; error is monotonically decreasing in lambda." />
  <figcaption><strong>Faithfulness vs. utility.</strong> <em>Top:</em> downstream LDS peaks at an <em>intermediate</em> $\lambda^\star$ on every setting — sharply on MNIST + LR, more broadly on MLP and ResNet9. <em>Bottom:</em> approximation error $\varepsilon_\lambda$ keeps shrinking as $\lambda$ grows, so the $\lambda$ that minimizes $\varepsilon_\lambda$ is <em>not</em> the $\lambda$ that maximizes LDS.</figcaption>
</figure>

This is exactly the pattern that breaks the JL framing: stronger $\lambda$ is *safer* for sketching but *worse* for the downstream task once the curvature signal washes out.

### A two-stage hyperparameter recipe

Putting the two halves together motivates a clean *faithfulness–utility* split:

1. **Choose $\lambda^\star$ for utility.** On a small validation set with $m$ large, sweep $\lambda$ and pick the $\lambda^\star$ that maximizes the downstream metric — typically an intermediate value (above).
2. **Choose $m$ for faithfulness.** Fix $\lambda = \lambda^\star$ and grow $m$ until $m \gtrsim C \cdot d_{\lambda^\star}(F)$ with $C \in (10, 100)$.

  <img src="/research/Projection-IF/Figures/hyperparam_selection.png" alt="Left: LDS versus lambda for three settings, with a star marker on the lambda* that maximizes LDS in each. Right: held-out LDS versus normalized sketch size m over d_lambda*(F); square markers indicate where each setting first reaches 95% of its max LDS, all within the 10–100 region." />
  <figcaption><strong>The recipe in practice.</strong> <em>Left:</em> validation-set sweep over $\lambda$ picks $\lambda^\star$ (stars). <em>Right:</em> held-out LDS vs. $m/d_{\lambda^\star}(F)$ — square markers mark where each setting first hits $95\%$ of its max LDS. On all three datasets, $m \in (10, 100) \cdot d_{\lambda^\star}(F)$ suffices, making the instance-adaptive bound directly operational.</figcaption>

The first stage is sample-efficient (small validation set, no need for tight sketches); the second is principled (governed by an instance-adaptive quantity, not a worst-case rank).

<div class="takeaway">

**Takeaway.** Influence is *not a distance*, so JL is the wrong tool to justify sketching it. The right complexity parameter is the **effective dimension** $d_\lambda(F)$ at the regularization scale — instance-adaptive, often orders of magnitude smaller than the rank. Three settings (unregularized, regularized, factorized) all fall out of the same resolvent perturbation argument, and the operational recipe is simple: pick $\lambda^\star$ on a small validation set, then scale $m$ to $10\text{–}100 \cdot d_{\lambda^\star}(F)$.

</div>
