## Problem

Training data fundamentally shapes what a model can do, what it gets wrong, and what biases it inherits — yet as generative AI has scaled, pinning down *which data drives which behavior* has become both more consequential and harder to answer. A body of work on **data attribution** has grown to address this, but it has developed unevenly: methods, application domains, and evaluation protocols have each accumulated their own conventions with little cross-talk. The field lacks a unified treatment that brings these threads together.

## Key Idea

The survey organizes the entire literature around the **What-What-How Attribution Framework** — three questions that any attribution problem must answer: *what to attribute* (the model behavior of interest), *attribute to what* (the training entities held responsible), and *how to attribute* (the influence measure used to assign credit). This framing cuts across all method families and application settings, giving the field a common vocabulary and exposing structural similarities that domain-specific reviews have obscured.

## Approach

Under that framework the survey proceeds on three fronts. First, it systematically covers the major attribution method families: **influence functions** (gradient-based approximations of leave-one-out retraining), **weighted marginal contributions** (data-Shapley and related cooperative-game estimators), **training dynamics** methods (tracing data effects through the learning trajectory), and **simulators** (surrogate models that predict counterfactual outcomes). Second, it maps these methods onto their principal applications — data selection and curation, fact tracing and source identification, adversarial attacks and defenses, and the emerging **data economy** (valuation, licensing, and compensation). Third, it examines the evaluation landscape, assessing how well attributors predict counterfactual outcomes, whether those predictions translate to downstream utility, and what the computational cost looks like at generative-AI scale.

## Result

The survey closes with a forward-looking account of what the field still needs. Open challenges include scaling attribution faithfully to large generative models, handling the multi-step attribution chains that arise in instruction-tuned and RLHF-trained systems, and establishing evaluation benchmarks that reflect real-world utility rather than proxy metrics. The authors identify the data economy as a particularly fertile direction: as data becomes a tradeable asset, principled attribution methods will be essential for valuation, provenance, and fair compensation.
