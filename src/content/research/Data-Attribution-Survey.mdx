**TL;DR.** Data attribution has fragmented into method-specific dialects, application-specific conventions, and incompatible evaluation protocols. We organize the entire field around a single spine — the **What-What-How Attribution Framework**: *what to attribute* (model behavior), *attribute to what* (training entities), and *how to attribute* (influence measures) — and use it to systematize methods, applications, and evaluation in the era of generative AI.

Training data is the fuel of modern AI: it sets what a model can do, where it fails, and which biases it carries. As generative models scaled, the question *which data drives which behavior?* became simultaneously more consequential and more technically demanding. A decade of work on **data attribution** has grown to address it, but methods, applications, and evaluation protocols have each accumulated their own conventions with little cross-talk. This survey gives the field a common vocabulary, a structural map, and an honest accounting of what is — and is not — known.

## Why this survey

- **A unified vocabulary across the entire literature.** Every attribution problem reduces to three questions. Fixing this framing dissolves apparent disagreements between method families and exposes structural similarities that domain-specific reviews have obscured.
- **Generative-AI scope as the organizing constraint.** Scale, multi-stage training (pre-training, SFT, RLHF, RLVR), and new behaviors (fact tracing, in-context learning, memorization, copyright) reshape every component of the framework — not just the algorithms, but the *targets* and *entities* themselves.
- **Evaluation gets its own seat at the table.** We treat counterfactual prediction, downstream utility, and compute cost as separable axes rather than a single number — because in practice attribution methods that win on one often lose on the others.
- **The data economy as a first-class application.** Attribution is no longer just a debugging tool; it is the technical substrate of data valuation, copyright compensation, and equitable data markets. We treat this seriously rather than tacking it on as an afterthought.

## The What-What-How Framework

Formally:

$$
\text{Data attribution measures the } \underbrace{\text{influence}}_{\text{\textit{how}}} \text{ of each } \underbrace{\text{training entity}}_{\text{\textit{to what}}} \text{ on the } \underbrace{\text{model behavior}}_{\text{\textit{what}}}.
$$

<figure class="figure-block">
  <img src="/research/Data-Attribution-Survey/Figures/method-overview.png" alt="Three-component cycle of data attribution: training entities D feed into the trained model theta, which exhibits model behaviors f(theta); the influence measure quantifies how each training entity contributes to those behaviors, closing the loop back to the entities." />
  <figcaption><strong>The three components.</strong> ① <em>Training entities</em> $\mathcal{D}$ produce a trained model $\theta$; ② the trained model exhibits <em>model behaviors</em> $f(\theta)$ — test predictions, activated neurons, loss trajectories; ③ the <em>influence measure</em> $\tau(z_i, \mathcal{S}; f)$ assigns each entity a score for the behavior of interest. Picking different choices on each axis recovers most of the literature.</figcaption>
</figure>

The three components are conceptually orthogonal: a method choice (*how*) can be paired with many different entity granularities (*to what*) and many different target functions (*what*). Most confusion in the literature comes from collapsing these dimensions.

### What to attribute — model behavior

The target $f(\theta)$ encodes *what we care about*. It is static or dynamic, scalar or set-valued, but it must be measurable from the trained (or training) model. The advent of generative AI radically widens the catalog:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Model family | Common target functions |
| --- | --- |
| Decoder-only LLMs | Negative log-likelihood; multi-class margin |
| Diffusion models | DDPM loss; noise-prediction norm; per-timestep prediction |
| CLIP / multimodal | Contrastive loss |
| Beyond loss | Fact recall, in-context learning, memorization, safety/toxicity, generation quality |

</div>

Many emergent behaviors (fact tracing, hallucination, RLHF preference alignment) lack a clean closed-form target and are instead probed through *proxy* targets — a gap the survey flags as a first-class open problem.

### Attribute to what — training entity

The entity is the *unit of credit*. The literature uses five granularities; the choice is dictated by the application and the available structure in the data.

<div class="table-scroll">

| Granularity | Example use |
| --- | --- |
| Token | Per-token influence in LLM generation |
| Concept | Phrases, passages, contextual structures |
| Sample | Default — single training example |
| Group | Subset influence under additivity |
| Domain | Whole datasets or source corpora (e.g., DoReMi, AutoScale, RegMix) |

</div>

A subtle but important distinction: most methods *compute* per-sample scores and *aggregate* upward under an additivity assumption. When additivity fails — as it does for highly correlated or duplicated samples — aggregation breaks, and group/domain attribution must be computed natively.

### How to attribute — influence measure

The influence measure $\tau(z_i, \mathcal{S}; f)$ is the channel that links entities to behavior. We categorize the literature into four families plus a residual class:

- **Influence functions (IF).** First-order approximations of leave-one-out retraining. The classical form $\hat\theta_{-z} - \hat\theta \approx \tfrac{1}{n}\,H_{\hat\theta}^{-1}\nabla_\theta \ell(z;\hat\theta)$, scaled through Hessian approximations (FIM, GGN, K-FAC, EK-FAC, DataInf) and parameter/sample reduction. TRAK reframes IF as a linear datamodel via projected gradients on a logistic surrogate.
- **Weighted marginal contributions (WMC).** Cooperative-game estimators — Data Shapley, Beta Shapley, Data Banzhaf, weighted Banzhaf — unified as **probabilistic values** $\phi_i = \sum_k \alpha_k^{(n)} \sum_{|S|=k-1} \Delta(z_i, U, S)$ with weights $\alpha_k^{(n)} = \int_0^1 t^{k-1}(1-t)^{n-k}\,d\xi(t)$.
- **Training-dynamics methods.** Use intermediate checkpoints. Two sub-families: *tracing gradient descent* (TracIn and its ideal limit, accumulating $\eta_t \langle \nabla_\theta f(z_\text{test}), \nabla_\theta \ell(z) \rangle$ along the trajectory), and *trajectory-specific LOO* (SGD-influence and its dynamic LOO descendants).
- **Simulators.** Surrogate models — Datamodels with $g_\theta(\mathbf{1}_S) \approx f_\mathcal{A}(z;S)$, plus DVRL, RegMix, Simfluence, GPTfluence — trained directly to predict counterfactual outcomes from subset specifications or training curricula.
- **Other approaches.** Unlearning-based, similarity-based, and representation-based attribution that fit none of the above.

A summary of the trade-offs:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Family | Cost on GenAI | Strengths | Failure modes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Influence functions | Medium–high | Principled LOO approximation; rich theory | iHVP cost; non-convexity, non-convergence |
| Weighted marginal contributions | Prohibitive | Game-theoretic axioms; native group/domain | Exponential subset enumeration |
| Training dynamics (TracIn) | Low | Trivially scalable; no Hessian | Biased toward easy / short samples |
| Simulators (Datamodels) | High (offline) | Direct fit to counterfactuals | Requires many retrains; misspecification |

</div>

## Methods, applications, and evaluation

### Method families in detail

Within each family, the survey is explicit about *how scaling to generative AI changes the algorithm*, not just the runtime. Concretely:

- **For IF:** the iHVP $H_{\hat\theta}^{-1} v$ at $O(np^2 + p^3)$ runtime is the bottleneck. Three orthogonal axes attack it — restricting parameters (last-layer, MLP-only, LoRA-only), filtering samples (TF-IDF, kNN, batch queries), and approximating the iHVP (block-diagonal FIM, K-FAC, EK-FAC, DataInf, LiSSA, gradient projection). The same techniques transfer to TracIn and TRAK, which is why we treat them within a shared complexity table.
- **For WMC:** the central question is *how many subset evaluations $T$ to achieve an $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-approximation*. Permutation sampling gives $O(n^2 \log n)$, group testing $O(n(\log n)^2)$, MSR (for Banzhaf) $O(n \log n)$, OFA $O(n \log n)$ across all semivalues. The exponential dependence on $|S|$ kills naive Shapley at GenAI scale; this is why WMC is mostly relegated to domain-level or feature-level attribution in modern work.
- **For training dynamics:** the bottleneck is per-sample gradient materialization. Ghost dot-product factorizes $\langle \nabla_\theta \ell^{(1)}, \nabla_\theta \ell^{(2)} \rangle = \langle a^{(1)}, a^{(2)}\rangle \cdot \langle \partial\ell^{(1)}/\partial s^{(1)}, \partial\ell^{(2)}/\partial s^{(2)}\rangle$ for linear layers — eliminating gradient instantiation. This is the structural insight behind in-run Data Shapley, GREATS, and a slew of follow-ups.
- **For simulators:** the offline cost is "$M$ retrains" — manageable for CIFAR-scale diffusion (TRAK uses tens to thousands of ensemble models) but unattainable for LLM pre-training. Hence the shift to proxy-model datamodels (DsDm) and trajectory simulators (Simfluence, GPTfluence) that amortize across many test points.

### Applications

The survey covers four primary application clusters; each instantiates the framework with different target functions and entity granularities.

<div class="table-scroll">

| Application | Typical target $f$ | Typical entity | Representative methods |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Data selection / curation | Validation loss or self-loss | Sample, domain | LESS, GREATS, MATES, DsDm, RegMix, AutoScale |
| Fact tracing & concept probing | Token likelihood, factual recall | Sample, concept | Causal attribution beyond n-gram matching; ContextCite |
| Adversarial attacks & defenses | Loss / behavior on target | Sample | Poisoning detection, backdoor identification |
| Data economy | Log-likelihood, MMD, utility | Sample, dataset | Data Shapley + variants for valuation and royalties; FairShare; LAVA |

</div>

A distinctive emphasis: the **data economy** thread treats attribution as the technical primitive for *data valuation* (who contributed what?) and *data compensation* (how to pay them?) — including buyer-side market dynamics, privacy-preserving evaluation, and synthetic-data rewards in collaborative generative modeling.

### Evaluation landscape

Evaluations split along three axes:

- **Counterfactual prediction of model behavior.** Does $\tau$ predict what happens under retraining? Sub-protocols: LOO (Pearson with $\ell(z_\text{test}; \hat\theta_{\mathcal{S}\setminus\{z_i\}}) - \ell(z_\text{test}; \hat\theta_\mathcal{S})$); **Linear Datamodeling Score (LDS)**, which is Spearman correlation between attribution-based predictions $g_\tau(z, \mathcal{S}';\mathcal{S}) = \sum_{i: z_i \in \mathcal{S}'} \tau(z_i, \mathcal{S}; f)$ and ground-truth $f(z;\hat\theta_{\mathcal{S}'})$ over random subsets; and **trajectory-specific LOO** for the dynamic regime.
- **Utility in downstream tasks.** Does $\tau$ steer model behavior when used to add/remove training data? Metrics: top-$k$ / bottom-$k$ removal effects, accuracy drops, FID changes for diffusion, **brittleness** (the minimum $|\mathcal{S}'|$ such that $f(z;\hat\theta_{\mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{S}'}) \neq y_z$).
- **Computational cost.** Pre-compute vs. per-query cost; offline vs. online; storage footprint; ensemble multiplier. At GenAI scale this axis is no longer ignorable — a method that needs 1000 retrains is qualitatively different from one that needs a single backward pass.

A recurring caveat: LDS and brittleness both rely on an **additivity assumption** on $\tau$. When attribution is genuinely non-additive (correlated samples, group effects), high LDS may not translate to downstream utility — and a method with lower LDS may dominate in selection or unlearning tasks.

## Open challenges

- **Scale as a first-class consideration.** A *scale-aware mindset* must permeate GenAI attribution — both as a constraint on algorithms (efficient IF, in-run Shapley, gradient projection) and as a phenomenon to study (how does data influence itself scale with $n$, $p$, $T$?).
- **Heterogeneous data and multi-stage paradigms.** Frontier LLMs are continual artifacts (pre-training $\to$ SFT $\to$ RLHF / RLVR), trained on text + image + synthetic + preference data. Attribution must answer *cumulative* questions across stages — e.g., "how much value does this new data point add to the existing model?" — and handle modality mixtures and synthetic-data contamination.
- **Attribution as a tool for responsible AI.** Bias, privacy leakage, copyright traceability, hallucination diagnosis: each requires an attribution component. The survey argues that data attribution should be treated as foundational infrastructure for AI governance, not a niche interpretability tool.
- **Proactive and dynamic attribution.** Move from post-hoc analysis to integrated training-time use: real-time sampling, weighting, and filtering driven by influence scores.
- **Application-oriented benchmarks.** LOO and LDS can be fooled and are noisy at scale. The field needs benchmarks that capture the complexity of real GenAI applications (fact tracing on web-scale corpora, RLHF reward shaping, copyright disputes) with standardized protocols (multi-seed, ensembled ground truth).
- **Synergy with mechanistic interpretability.** Data attribution explains the *training-side* of model behavior; mechanistic interpretability explains the *parameter-side*. Combining the two is a clear path to comprehensive understanding of generative models.
