**TL;DR.** **GraSS** is a two-stage per-sample-gradient compression pipeline — *sparsify* (random or learned mask) then *sparse-project* (SJLT) — that drives both time and space cost from $O(pk)$ to $O(k')$ with $k < k' \ll p$, *sub-linear* in model size. Its linear-layer variant **FactGraSS** never materializes the full gradient, runs at **72,218 tokens/sec** on Llama-3.1-8B (vs. **27,292** for [LoGra](https://openreview.net/forum?id=zPKeJAEo27)), and is backed by a custom CUDA kernel for SJLT.

Gradient-based data attribution — influence functions, [TRAK](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14186), and friends — needs per-sample gradients $g_i = \nabla_\theta \ell(z_i; \hat\theta)$ for every training point. For $n$ samples and $p$ parameters that is $O(np)$ memory and compute; even after compressing each gradient to $k \ll p$ dimensions, dense random projection still costs $O(nkp)$. GraSS attacks this bottleneck by exploiting a structural property the literature has mostly ignored: *per-sample gradients are inherently sparse*, and sparsity in the input is precisely what the Sparse Johnson–Lindenstrauss Transform was built to exploit.

## Why GraSS

- **Two complementary sparsities.** Per-sample gradients are sparse because of ReLU-style activations (sparse pre-activations propagate through the chain rule); networks also exhibit *effective parameter sparsity*, so a small subset of coordinates carries most of the signal. GraSS turns both into a sub-linear algorithm.
- **Sub-linear in $p$.** End-to-end per-sample cost is $O(k')$ with $k < k' \ll p$, strictly better than dense projection $O(pk)$, [FJLT](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/~chazelle/pubs/FJLT-sicomp09.pdf) $O((p+k)\log p)$, and [LoGra](https://openreview.net/forum?id=zPKeJAEo27) $O(\sqrt{p_l k_l})$ at any realistic compression ratio.
- **Custom SJLT CUDA kernel.** Beats hardware-tuned dense matmul at the projection sizes that actually arise — small-$k$ regime, where PyTorch's `bmm` is launch-bound. We open-source it at [TRAIS-Lab/sjlt](https://github.com/TRAIS-Lab/sjlt).
- **Drop-in for TRAK and influence functions.** The compression is agnostic to the downstream attribution method: TRAK on MLP/ResNet/Music Transformer, layer-wise block-diagonal influence on GPT-2 and Llama-3.1-8B all benefit.
- **It moves the numbers.** On Llama-3.1-8B + 1B OpenWebText tokens, **FactGraSS** delivers **72,218 tok/s** of compression vs. **27,292** for LoGra (**+165%**), and **8,584 tok/s** for the full caching stage vs. **7,307** (**+17%**). On GPT-2 + WikiText, FactGraSS matches LoGra's LDS at a **3.2×** smaller wall-time.

## Method

### Per-sample gradient sparsity

<figure class="figure-block figure-right">
  <img src="/research/GraSS/Figures/SJLT.png" alt="SJLT viewed as a column scan: each nonzero g(j) (highlighted in pink on the left) sends one +1 or -1 contribution to a random target coordinate of hat_g, accumulated into the length-k output." />
  <figcaption><strong>SJLT as a column scan.</strong> Initialize $\hat g = 0$; for every $j$ with $g(j) \neq 0$, push $\pm g(j)$ into a random output coordinate. Total work scales with $\operatorname{nnz}(g)$, *independent of $k$*.</figcaption>
</figure>

Consider a first linear layer $h = \operatorname{ReLU}(Wz)$ with $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{out}} \times d_{\mathrm{in}}}$. Because $\operatorname{ReLU}(x) = \max(0, x)$ zeroes every negative pre-activation, the chain rule pushes those zeros straight into $\nabla_W \ell(z; \hat\theta)$. The same holds for any activation with a vanishing region — GELU, SiLU, and so on at saturation. Critically, this is a *per-sample* phenomenon: averaging $g_i$ over a mini-batch immediately destroys the pattern, which is why standard training pipelines never see it.

The Sparse Johnson–Lindenstrauss Transform ($\operatorname{SJLT}$) of [Dasgupta–Kumar–Sarlós](https://arxiv.org/abs/1006.3585) and [Kane–Nelson](https://arxiv.org/abs/1006.3585) keeps only $s = o_\epsilon(k)$ nonzero entries per column of the projection matrix. Viewed as a column-scan, applying $\operatorname{SJLT}$ to a vector $g$ touches $s \cdot \operatorname{nnz}(g)$ entries — *linear in the input's nonzero count*, *independent of $k$*:

$$
\operatorname{SJLT}_k(g) \in \mathbb{R}^k,\qquad \text{time} = O(s \cdot \operatorname{nnz}(g)),\ s = o_\epsilon(k).
$$

We default to $s = 1$, which still preserves pair-wise distances with small empirical error and unlocks the largest speedups.

### Sparsification: random and selective masks

<figure class="figure-block figure-right">
  <img src="/research/GraSS/Figures/Mask.png" alt="Random/Selective Mask: a length-p gradient g has only k coordinates pulled into the output hat_g (highlighted in pink), via a sparse binary selection matrix with one nonzero per row." />
  <figcaption><strong>$\operatorname{Mask}_k$ as index extraction.</strong> The selection matrix has exactly one nonzero per row — uniform ($\operatorname{RM}_k$) or learned ($\operatorname{SM}_k$). Cost $O(k)$, *sub-linear in $p$*.</figcaption>
</figure>

To go below $O(p)$, GraSS first applies a *sparsification mask* $\operatorname{Mask}_{k'} \colon \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^{k'}$, an index-extraction onto a $k'$-dimensional subspace. Two variants:

- ***Random Mask*** $\operatorname{RM}_{k'}$: pick $k'$ coordinates uniformly at random. Cost is $O(k')$, sub-linear in $p$. Surprisingly competitive when the underlying gradient is sparse or the model is over-parameterized.
- ***Selective Mask*** $\operatorname{SM}_{k'}$: learn the mask by solving a continuous relaxation over $S \in \mathbb{R}^p$:

  $$
  S^\ast = \arg\max_{S \in \mathbb{R}^p} \mathbb{E}_{z_{\mathrm{test}}}\!\left[\operatorname{corr}\!\big((\langle g_i, g_{z_{\mathrm{test}}}\rangle)_{i=1}^n,\, (\langle \hat g_i, \hat g_{z_{\mathrm{test}}}\rangle)_{i=1}^n\big)\right] \;-\; \lambda \lVert \sigma(S) \rVert_1,
  $$

  with $\hat g_i = \sigma(S) \odot g_i$. The first term aligns the masked attribution scores ([GradDot](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper_files/paper/2019/hash/c61f571dbd2fb949d3fe5ae1608dd48b-Abstract.html)) with the unmasked ones; the $\ell_1$ penalty drives $\sigma(S)$ towards a binary indicator. Thresholding $\sigma(S^\ast) \geq 0.5$ recovers the final binary mask $M \in \{0,1\}^{k' \times p}$. Solving (one-time) is first-order; using $M$ at attribution time is again $O(k')$.

### GraSS: sparsify, then sparse-project

Compose the two:

$$
\operatorname{GraSS}_k(g) \;\coloneqq\; \operatorname{SJLT}_k \circ \operatorname{Mask}_{k'}(g),\qquad k < k' \ll p.
$$

The sparsification step costs $O(k')$ and yields a $k'$-dimensional vector that is *fully dense* in its own coordinates but $k'$-sparse with respect to the original $p$ dimensions. SJLT then runs in $O(s\,k') = O(k')$ with $s=1$. Total per-sample cost is $O(k')$ — strictly sub-linear in $p$. Two extremes recover known methods: $k' = p$ gives vanilla SJLT; $k' = k$ gives standalone sparsification. The hyperparameter $k'$ is the explicit knob trading fidelity against speed.

The following table summarizes the per-sample compression cost for all the methods we compare against:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Method | Notation | Per-sample cost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Dense Gaussian | $\operatorname{Gauss}_k$ | $O(pk)$ |
| [FJLT](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/~chazelle/pubs/FJLT-sicomp09.pdf) ([TRAK](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14186)'s default) | $\operatorname{FJLT}_k$ | $O((p+k)\log p)$ |
| Sparsification | $\operatorname{Mask}_k$ | $O(k)$ |
| Sparse projection | $\operatorname{SJLT}_k$ | $O(p)$ |
| **GraSS** | $\operatorname{SJLT}_k \circ \operatorname{Mask}_{k'}$ | $O(k')$ |

</div>

### FactGraSS: factorized GraSS for linear layers

<figure class="figure-block">
  <img src="/research/GraSS/Figures/LoGra.png" alt="LoGra for one linear layer: the forward input z_in and the backward gradient signal Dz_out are projected separately by P_in and P_out into a k_in by k_out factor, whose outer product forms the compressed gradient hat_g_il." />
  <figcaption><strong>LoGra baseline.</strong> The gradient $\operatorname{vec}(\mathcal{D} W_l)$ factorizes as $\sum_t (z_{i,l}^{\mathrm{in}})_{:t} \otimes \mathcal{D}(z_{i,l}^{\mathrm{out}})_{:t}$; <a href="https://openreview.net/forum?id=zPKeJAEo27">LoGra</a> projects the two factors independently with dense Gaussian matrices $P_l^{\mathrm{in}}, P_l^{\mathrm{out}}$, never materializing the full gradient. Cost: $O(\sqrt{p_l k_l})$ per layer.</figcaption>
</figure>

Linear layers dominate the parameter count and gradient cost of transformers; influence-function pipelines typically assume a layer-wise *block-diagonal* FIM and apply compression per layer. For the $l$-th linear layer with weight $W_l \in \mathbb{R}^{d_l^{\mathrm{out}} \times d_l^{\mathrm{in}}}$, input $z_{i,l}^{\mathrm{in}}$, and pre-activation gradient $\mathcal{D} z_{i,l}^{\mathrm{out}}$, the gradient is a sum of outer products:

$$
\operatorname{vec}(\mathcal{D} W_l) \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \big(z_{i,l}^{\mathrm{in}}\big)_{:t} \otimes \mathcal{D}\big(z_{i,l}^{\mathrm{out}}\big)_{:t}.
$$

[LoGra](https://openreview.net/forum?id=zPKeJAEo27) exploits this Kronecker structure by factorizing the projection as $P_l = P_l^{\mathrm{in}} \otimes P_l^{\mathrm{out}}$ and projecting the two activation streams *separately*, achieving $O(\sqrt{p_l k_l})$ per layer without ever materializing $\mathcal{D} W_l$. Plugging GraSS naively into this template fails on two fronts:

- Per-layer problem sizes shrink to roughly $\sqrt{p_l} \times \sqrt{k_l}$, the regime where SJLT is *slower* than dense matmul because it is launch-bound and cache-unfriendly.
- Reconstructing the full $p_l$-dimensional gradient to dodge the small-problem regime would cost $O(p_l)$ and erase the win.

**FactGraSS** (right panel of the hero) threads the needle: sparsify the *factors* independently, then reconstruct only the *sparsified gradient*. Concretely, given a target $k_l = k_l^{\mathrm{in}} \times k_l^{\mathrm{out}}$ for layer $l$:

1. **Sparsification.** Mask $z_{i,l}^{\mathrm{in}}$ and $\mathcal{D} z_{i,l}^{\mathrm{out}}$ to intermediate dimensions ${k_l^{\mathrm{in}}}^{\prime}$ and ${k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}^{\prime}$ respectively, with $k_l^{\mathrm{in}} \leq {k_l^{\mathrm{in}}}^{\prime} \ll d_l^{\mathrm{in}}$.
2. **Reconstruction.** Form the sparsified gradient $g_{i,l}'$ of dimension $k_l' \coloneqq {k_l^{\mathrm{in}}}^{\prime} \times {k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}^{\prime}$ via Kronecker product of the two masked factors.
3. **Sparse projection.** Apply $\operatorname{SJLT}_{k_l}$ to $g_{i,l}'$ to land at the target dimension.

Total per-layer cost: $O({k_l^{\mathrm{in}}}^{\prime}) + O({k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}^{\prime}) + O(k_l') = O(k_l')$. We write FactGraSS as $\operatorname{SJLT}_{k_l} \circ \operatorname{Mask}_{{k_l^{\mathrm{in}}}^{\prime} \otimes {k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}^{\prime}}$, where $\otimes$ flags the factorized sparsification. Writing $k_l' = c \cdot k_l$ for a *blow-up factor* $c \geq 1$, FactGraSS beats LoGra whenever

$$
k_l' \;\leq\; \sqrt{k_l \cdot p_l} \;\iff\; c \;\leq\; \sqrt{p_l / k_l}.
$$

For a typical Llama-style layer with $p_l = 4096 \times 4096$ and $k_l = 64 \times 64$, any blow-up factor $c \leq 64$ works — easy to satisfy. Aggregating over layers with $k' \coloneqq \sum_l k_l'$, the network-level cost is $O(k')$, the same sub-linear bound as GraSS but *without ever materializing $\nabla_{W_l} \ell$*.

## System: a CUDA kernel for SJLT

A textbook PyTorch implementation of SJLT loses to dense `bmm` at the problem sizes we actually need. Two root causes:

- **Thread contention.** Multiple input threads write to the same output coordinate when $k$ is small, causing serializing atomic adds.
- **Irregular memory access.** SJLT's scatter pattern is cache-unfriendly compared to the highly tuned, fused multiply-accumulate paths in cuBLAS.

We wrote a custom CUDA kernel ([TRAIS-Lab/sjlt](https://github.com/TRAIS-Lab/sjlt)) that re-tiles the scatter to minimize hot-spot contention and aligns memory access along the output dimension. At $p = 131{,}072$ with $s = 1$, the kernel beats dense Rademacher and Gaussian projections in wall-time at every $k$ we tested while inheriting SJLT's linear scaling in $\operatorname{nnz}(g)$ — neither dense baseline exploits input sparsity at all. This is what makes the sub-linear theory translate into the throughput numbers below.

## Results

### LDS: GraSS matches sparse-projection LDS at sparsification cost

We measure attribution fidelity with the [Linear Datamodeling Score](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14186) (LDS) across four settings. All wall-times are on one NVIDIA A40.

**TRAK + ResNet-9 on CIFAR-2.** Standalone sparsification is fast but loses LDS; standalone SJLT recovers LDS at a steep wall-time. GraSS with $k' = 4 k_{\max}$ hits both targets:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Method ($k = 8192$) | LDS | Time (s) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| $\operatorname{RM}_k$ | 0.4236 | 0.1296 |
| $\operatorname{SM}_k$ | 0.4292 | **0.1134** |
| $\operatorname{SJLT}_k$ | 0.4747 | 17.48 |
| **$\operatorname{SJLT}_k \circ \operatorname{RM}_{4k_{\max}}$** | 0.4545 | 0.3993 |
| **$\operatorname{SJLT}_k \circ \operatorname{SM}_{4k_{\max}}$** | 0.4581 | 0.2911 |
| $\operatorname{FJLT}_k$ (baseline) | **0.4753** | 81.93 |

</div>

*GraSS reaches FJLT-level LDS at roughly **280× faster** wall-time*; against dense $\operatorname{SJLT}_k$ alone, GraSS shaves about **60× off** while keeping comparable LDS. The same pattern holds for Music Transformer / MAESTRO and the MLP / MNIST ablation.

**Influence function + GPT-2 on WikiText.** Switching to layer-wise block-diagonal FIM with linear-layer factorization, FactGraSS competes with LoGra on LDS at a fraction of the cost:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Method ($k_l = 4096$) | LDS | Time (s) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| $\operatorname{RM}_{k_l^{\mathrm{in}} \otimes k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}$ | **0.2391** | **5.64** |
| $\operatorname{SM}_{k_l^{\mathrm{in}} \otimes k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}$ | 0.2267 | 5.83 |
| $\operatorname{SJLT}_{k_l^{\mathrm{in}} \otimes k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}$ | 0.2389 | 136.5 |
| **FactGraSS** ($\operatorname{SJLT}_{k_l} \circ \operatorname{RM}_{2k_l^{\mathrm{in}} \otimes 2k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}$) | 0.2360 | 6.31 |
| **FactGraSS** ($\operatorname{SJLT}_{k_l} \circ \operatorname{SM}_{2k_l^{\mathrm{in}} \otimes 2k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}$) | 0.2380 | 6.33 |
| $\operatorname{Gauss}_{k_l^{\mathrm{in}} \otimes k_l^{\mathrm{out}}}$ (LoGra) | 0.2338 | 22.22 |

</div>

*FactGraSS matches LoGra's LDS while running **3.5× faster***, and pulls within $5\times 10^{-3}$ of factorized SJLT at **22×** less wall-time.

### Qualitative attribution on Llama-3.1-8B

We filter out the well-known [gradient-norm outliers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.13954) (error logs, ASCII dumps, repetitive tokens) and inspect the top-10 most influential samples identified by FactGraSS at $k_l = 4096$. Prompted with *"To improve data privacy,"* the model emits a structured GDPR-style response, and FactGraSS surfaces a training paragraph from a news-site terms-of-service discussing account-level privacy enforcement, "right to delete," and personal-data handling — a topically aligned, non-trivial attribution that the gradient-norm baseline misses.
