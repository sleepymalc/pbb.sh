**TL;DR.** **Dr. Post-Training** recasts general training data as a *data-induced regularizer* and the model update as a Euclidean projection of the target gradient onto a training-batch-induced feasible set—turning data selection into one knob on a principled bias–variance spectrum.

Large language model (LLM) post-training operates on *scarce, high-fidelity target data* alongside abundant but imperfectly aligned general data. Existing work asks *which* general samples to keep; we ask *what constraint* they should impose on the update. The answer is a single projection step that subsumes standard training and prior data-selection methods as special cases, and exposes a richer design space — **Group-Wise Subset Update** — that consistently beats state-of-the-art baselines across SFT, RLHF, and RLVR.

## Why Dr. Post-Training

- **[One unified framework](#method).** A single projection-based update that subsumes standard training, target-only fine-tuning, and existing data-selection methods (top-$k$, thresholding, greedy curation) as special cases on one bias–variance spectrum — across SFT, RLHF, and RLVR.
- **[Group-Wise Subset Update](#our-proposal-group-wise-subset-update).** Strictly enlarges the feasible set over Global Subset — provably less bias.
- **[Plays well with the modern stack](#system-a-one-pass-memory-tight-implementation).** LoRA, [MeSO](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.03507), activation checkpointing, and gradient accumulation — no two-pass backward, no retained graph.
- **[It moves the numbers](#results).** SFT F1 on *less-mix/tydiqa* jumps from **10.1% → 27.4%**, with consistent gains across the RLHF detoxification and RLVR-on-MATH settings we tested.
- **[Overhead shrinks with scale](#system-efficiency-overhead-vanishes-with-scale).** Single forward–backward; the **34% → 3%** wall-clock overhead from SmolLM2-360M to LLaMA-3.2-3B keeps closing — pointing to break-even, or even a speedup, at production scale.

## Method

### Problem setup

At step $t$ the optimizer draws two batches: a training batch $B_t = \{z_i\}_{i=1}^n \sim \mathbb{P}_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and a (small) target batch $B_t^\star = \{z_j^\star\}_{j=1}^m \sim \mathbb{P}_\star$. Write the per-sample gradients $g_i, g_j^\star$ and their batch means $\hat g_{\mathrm{tr}}, \hat g_\star$. Standard post-training updates $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta_t u_t$, and our central modeling choice is to pick

$$
u_t \;=\; \arg\min_{u \in U_t}\, \mathcal{L}_\star(\theta_t - \eta_t u),
$$

where the *feasible set* $U_t$ is built from the training batch $B_t$ only, and the target batch $B_t^\star$ enters solely through the target loss $\mathcal{L}_\star$.

### From constrained optimization to a projection

Under a standard $\beta$-smoothness assumption on $\mathcal{L}_\star$, majorization–minimization on the quadratic upper bound and plugging in the target-batch estimate $\hat g_\star$ for the (unknown) population gradient $g_\star$ reduces this constrained optimization to a Euclidean projection:

$$
u_t \;=\; \arg\min_{u \in U_t}\, \lVert u - \hat g_\star \rVert^2 \;=\; \operatorname{Proj}_{U_t}(\hat g_\star).
$$

The geometry of $U_t$ is now the only design choice — and it is the *data regularizer*.

### Existing methods as special cases

Three familiar training paradigms drop out of the framework by choosing different feasible sets:

- ***Target-Only*** $U_t^\star = \mathbb{R}^d$. No constraint; $u_t = \hat g_\star$ recovers gradient descent on the target batch — unbiased but high-variance when $m$ is small.
- ***Full-Training*** $U_t^{\mathrm{tr}} = \{\hat g_{\mathrm{tr}}\}$. Singleton; $u_t = \hat g_{\mathrm{tr}}$ recovers standard SFT/RLHF — zero variance but a distribution-mismatch bias floor $\lVert g_{\mathrm{tr}} - g_\star \rVert^2$.
- ***Global Subset*** $U_t^{\mathrm{glob}}(k) = \{g_S : S \subseteq [n], \lvert S \rvert = k\}$. All $k$-sample averages of training gradients. Existing data-selection methods (Top-$k$, thresholding, GREATS-style greedy) are tractable approximations to the resulting subset optimization, since

  $$
  \min_{S} \lVert g_S - \hat g_\star \rVert^2 \;\equiv\; \min_S\, \lVert g_S \rVert^2 - \frac{2}{k}\sum_{i \in S} \langle g_i, \hat g_\star \rangle,
  $$

  which trades an *alignment* term $s_i := \langle g_i, \hat g_\star \rangle$ against a *redundancy* penalty.

### Our proposal: Group-Wise Subset Update

A single global subset $S$ shared across *all* parameters is overly restrictive: a sample useful for one layer may be useless for another. **Group-Wise Subset Update** relaxes this by partitioning parameters into groups $\mathcal{G} = \{G_p\}_{p=1}^P$ and letting each group pick its own subset:

$$
U_t^{\mathrm{grp}}(k; \mathcal{G}) = \prod_{p=1}^P \left\{ \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i \in S} g_i^{(p)} : \lvert S \rvert = k \right\}.
$$

Because the projection factorizes, each group solves an independent top-$k$ on its own score $s_i^{(p)} := \langle g_i^{(p)}, \hat g_\star^{(p)} \rangle$. The practically important case is **Layer-Wise Subset Update**, with one group per linear layer.

The chain $U_t^{\mathrm{glob}}(k) \subseteq U_t^{\mathrm{grp}}(k;\mathcal{G}) \subseteq U_t^\star = \mathbb{R}^d$ orders all four families by expressiveness; the partition $\mathcal{G}$ and subset size $k$ are the explicit design knobs that move along the regularization spectrum.

### Bias–variance characterization

<figure class="figure-block">
  <img src="/research/Dr-Post-Training/Figures/spectrum.png" alt="Data-regularization spectrum: Target-Only, Group-Wise Subset, Global Subset, and Full-Training arranged from weakest to strongest regularization, trading variance for bias." />
  <figcaption><strong>Data-regularization spectrum.</strong> Four feasible sets, ordered by how much they constrain the update. Moving right (stronger data regularization) reduces variance at the cost of bias; moving left does the reverse. The two subset families occupy the middle, and the partition $\mathcal{G}$ and subset size $k$ slide along the axis.</figcaption>
</figure>

We measure the quality of an update $u$ at step $t$ by its **conditional mean-squared error** against the unknown population target gradient $g_\star = g_\star(\theta_t)$,

$$
\mathrm{MSE}(u) \;\coloneqq\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[\lVert u - g_\star \rVert^2 \,\bigm|\, \theta_t\bigr],
$$

where $g_\star$ is fixed given $\theta_t$ and the expectation is over the joint randomness in the training batch $B_t$ and the target batch $B_t^\star$. **Why bound MSE?** Because majorization–minimization turns it directly into per-step progress on the target loss:

<div class="lemma">

**Lemma** (Majorization). *Under a $\beta$-smooth target loss with $\eta_t \leq 1/\beta$,*

$$
\mathbb{E}\bigl[\mathcal{L}_\star(\theta_{t+1}) \,\bigm|\, \theta_t\bigr] \;\leq\; \mathcal{L}_\star(\theta_t) - \frac{\eta_t}{2}\lVert g_\star \rVert^2 + \frac{\eta_t}{2}\,\mathrm{MSE}(u_t).
$$

</div>

The *expected* per-step decrease on the target loss is therefore lower-bounded by $\tfrac{\eta_t}{2}\!\left(\lVert g_\star\rVert^2 - \mathrm{MSE}(u_t)\right)$ — a single scalar that turns each data regularizer into a guaranteed-progress rate, so the whole spectrum can be compared on one axis.

The MSE splits cleanly into a *bias* $\mathcal{B}(U_t) \coloneqq \mathbb{E}[\inf_{u \in U_t}\lVert u - g_\star \rVert^2 \mid \theta_t]$ — a property of the data regularizer alone, averaged over the training-batch randomness — and a residual *variance* $\mathcal{V}(u_t) \coloneqq \mathrm{MSE}(u_t) - \mathcal{B}(U_t)$, the extra error from picking the update inside $U_t$ using the noisy target-batch estimate $\hat g_\star$ in place of the unknown $g_\star$. By construction $\mathrm{MSE}(u_t) = \mathcal{B}(U_t) + \mathcal{V}(u_t)$, and we can pin down both halves for every method on the spectrum. Write $\Sigma_{\mathrm{tr}} \coloneqq \mathrm{Cov}(g_i)$ and $\Sigma_\star \coloneqq \mathrm{Cov}(g_j^\star)$.

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Bias–variance characterization, informal). *Under a $\beta$-smooth target loss, bounded training gradients $\lVert g_i \rVert \leq C$, and sub-Gaussian target noise with parameter $\sigma/\sqrt m$:*

- ***Target-Only*** *is unbiased, $\mathcal{B}(U_t^{\star}) = 0$, but $\mathcal{V}(u_t^{\star}) = \operatorname{tr}(\Sigma_\star)/m$.*
- ***Full-Training*** *has zero variance, $\mathcal{V}(u_t^{\mathrm{tr}}) = 0$, but carries the bias floor $\mathcal{B}(U_t^{\mathrm{tr}}) = \lVert g_{\mathrm{tr}} - g_\star \rVert^2 + \operatorname{tr}(\Sigma_{\mathrm{tr}})/n$.*
- ***Global Subset*** *trades both: bias has no closed form, while the variance satisfies $\mathcal{V}(u_t^{\mathrm{glob}}(k)) \leq \tfrac{4C\sigma}{\sqrt m}\sqrt{2\log(2\binom{n}{k})}$.*
- ***Group-Wise Subset*** *strictly improves the Global Subset bias, $\mathcal{B}(U_t^{\mathrm{grp}}(k;\mathcal{G})) \leq \mathcal{B}(U_t^{\mathrm{glob}}(k))$, at the price of $P\times$ in the variance bound, $\mathcal{V}(u_t^{\mathrm{grp}}(k;\mathcal{G})) \leq \tfrac{4CP\sigma}{\sqrt m}\sqrt{2\log(2\binom{n}{k})}$.*

</div>

Three patterns govern the spectrum:

- **The two extremes are mirror images.** Target-Only is unbiased but its variance $\operatorname{tr}(\Sigma_\star)/m$ blows up when the target batch is small. Full-Training has zero variance but carries an irreducible *distribution-mismatch bias* $\lVert g_{\mathrm{tr}} - g_\star \rVert^2$ that no amount of training data can erase.
- **Subset methods occupy the middle of the spectrum.** Both Global Subset and Group-Wise Subset improve the bias over Full-Training (their feasible set is strictly larger than $\{\hat g_{\mathrm{tr}}\}$) at the cost of $O(\sigma/\sqrt m \cdot \sqrt{\log\binom{n}{k}})$ extra variance — a logarithmic, not linear, dependence on the combinatorial complexity of the subset.
- **Refining the partition strictly reduces bias.** Because $U_t^{\mathrm{glob}}(k) \subseteq U_t^{\mathrm{grp}}(k;\mathcal{G})$, the group-wise bias is no larger; the variance bound only grows linearly in the number of groups $P$, so a layer-wise partition trades $P\times$ variance multiplier for a strictly smaller approximation error — and in practice the bias reduction dominates.

<div class="takeaway">

**Takeaway.** *Larger feasible sets reduce bias but increase variance*, since the update has more freedom to align both with $g_\star$ and **also with noise** $\xi$. Full-Training is stable but biased; Target-Only is unbiased but unstable when $m$ is small; Global and Group-Wise Subset Updates interpolate between them, with the partition $\mathcal{G}$ and subset size $k$ providing additional design knobs.

</div>

## System: a one-pass, memory-tight implementation

Two naive implementations make data-regularized updates impractical at LLM scale: a *two-pass* approach (run backward once for scoring, again for the assembled update — doubles runtime), and the *retain-graph* approach (`retain_graph=True` in PyTorch — keeps the whole autograd graph alive, blowing memory). We instead carefully manage the lifetime of a small number of intermediate tensors so the full data-regularized update fits in a *single* forward–backward pass with peak memory close to standard training.

### Standard training: what gets kept where

<figure class="figure-block">
  <img src="/research/Dr-Post-Training/Figures/standard.png" alt="Standard training forward and backward pass with per-layer memory footprint." />
  <figcaption><strong>Standard training.</strong> <em>Top row:</em> the per-layer computation graph for one Forward → Backward → Gradient step at layer $l$, and the start of Backward $l{-}1$. Solid arrows are explicit dependencies; dashed arrows cross the forward/backward boundary. <em>Bottom rows:</em> the lifetime (purple/orange bars) of the four key tensors — input activation $a^{(l)}$, pre-activation $e^{(l)}$, and the two backward signals $\partial \ell / \partial e^{(l)}$ and $\partial \ell / \partial a^{(l)}$. Each tensor is allocated and released as soon as its last consumer finishes, so the per-layer memory footprint stays roughly constant across the entire backward pass.</figcaption>
</figure>

Crucially, standard backprop *never materializes* the per-sample weight gradient $g_i^{(l)}$; it just folds it into the batch average. Our method needs $g_i^{(l)}$ twice — once for scoring, once for assembling the selected subset — which is exactly the dependency that breaks the standard schedule.

### Customized tensor lifetime scheduling

The key idea is a *swap*: at each layer, replace the forward-cached pre-activation $e^{(l)}$ with the backward-generated gradient $\partial \ell / \partial e^{(l)}$, which has the same shape and is no longer needed for upstream computation. The result is that, after the backward pass, every layer has the pair $(a^{(l)},\; \partial \ell / \partial e^{(l)})$ retained — the *minimal local information* from which both per-sample scores and per-sample gradients can be reconstructed on demand. We never form the full autograd graph, and we never run backward twice.

To compute both training and target gradients in one pass, we further merge the training batch and the target batch into a single forward, with $\ell \coloneqq \sum_{i=1}^n \ell(\theta_t; z_i) + \sum_{j=1}^m \ell(\theta_t; z_j^\star)$. Because samples don't share activations, the merged backward yields per-sample backward signals for *both* sets simultaneously.

When a group can be *finalized and released* depends on partition granularity, which gives a direct connection between the statistical and the systems design:

<figure class="figure-block">
  <img src="/research/Dr-Post-Training/Figures/compare.png" alt="Memory footprint comparison across Standard Training, Global Subset Update, and Layer-Wise Subset Update." />
  <figcaption><strong>Memory footprint comparison.</strong> Each row plots the temporal lifetime of the four key tensors across one optimization step; the bottom legend marks the four operation types — <span style="color:#9bbcd6"><strong>Forward</strong></span>, <span style="color:#c9a0a0"><strong>Backward</strong></span>, <span style="color:#e5d680"><strong>Gradient</strong></span>, <span style="color:#dc2626"><strong>Scoring</strong></span>. All three schemes hit a similar peak per layer. Global Subset Update must keep $(a^{(l)}, \partial \ell / \partial e^{(l)})$ alive across <em>every</em> layer until the global subset $S_t$ is determined (long pink/orange bars); Layer-Wise Subset resolves each layer in place and releases its tensors on-the-fly, almost matching the standard-training schedule.</figcaption>
</figure>

### Efficient per-sample scoring

The score $s_i^{(l)} = \langle \hat g_\star^{(l)},\, g_i^{(l)} \rangle$ can be evaluated in several ways with strikingly different cost profiles. Let $T$ be sequence length, $n$ training samples, $m$ target samples, $N = n+m$, and $\kappa \ll d/L$ the compressed gradient dimension.

<div class="table-scroll">

| Scoring method | FLOPs per layer | Memory per layer |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Direct** | $2NT(d/L) + n(d/L)$ | $(n+1)(d/L)$ |
| **GIP** (Ghost Inner Product) | $4nmT^2 \sqrt{d/L}$ | $2nmT^2$ |
| **PIP** (Per-Token Inner Product, new) | $2NT(d/L) + nT\sqrt{d/L}$ | $d/L + nT\sqrt{d/L}$ |
| **Compressed** (new) | $O(NT\kappa) + (2n+m)\kappa$ | $(n+1)\kappa$ |

</div>

**Direct** materializes per-sample gradients and pays an $O(n \cdot d/L)$ memory tax.
**GIP** ([Wang et al. 2024](https://openreview.net/forum?id=232VcN8tSx)) reorders the contraction to avoid forming $g_i^{(l)}$, but pays *quadratic* in sequence length $T$ — fatal for long-context training.
**PIP** factors the score as $s_i^{(l)} = \sum_\tau (\partial \ell / \partial e_{i,\tau}^{(l)})^\top \hat G_\star^{(l)}\, a_{i,\tau}^{(l)}$ — a matrix-vector product per token — so the cost stays *linear* in $T$ without materializing the full per-sample gradient. PIP dominates Direct whenever $T \lesssim \sqrt{d/L}$, and dominates GIP whenever $T$ is large.
**Compressed scoring** drops the $d/L$ dependence entirely by computing $\tilde g_i = \Pi^{(l)}(g_i^{(l)}) \in \mathbb{R}^\kappa$ via factorized projection ([GraSS](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.18976)), then approximates $s_i^{(l)} \approx \langle \tilde g_\star^{(l)},\, \tilde g_i^{(l)} \rangle$. Strictly more efficient than the three exact methods in every regime; we use it as the default.

### The full algorithm

Putting it all together, we now spell out **Layer-Wise Subset Update** — the representative case of Group-Wise Subset Update with one group per linear layer. Each layer scores its training samples, picks its top-$k$, and assembles its slice of $u_t$ in place during the backward sweep, releasing the retained pair $(a^{(\ell)}, \partial \ell / \partial e^{(\ell)})$ as soon as the layer is resolved.

<div class="algorithm">

**Algorithm.** Layer-Wise Subset Update — one optimization step.

**Input:** model $\theta_t$, training batch $B_t = \{z_i\}_{i=1}^n$, target batch $B_t^\star = \{z_j^\star\}_{j=1}^m$, subset size $k$, learning rate $\eta_t$.

**Output:** updated parameters $\theta_{t+1}$.

1. $(\ell,\; \{a^{(l)},\, e^{(l)}\}) \leftarrow \operatorname{Forward}(\theta_t,\, B_t \cup B_t^\star)$<span class="algorithm-comment">▹ merged batch, cache forward tensors</span>
2. $u_t \leftarrow 0$
3. **for** $\ell = L, \dots, 1$ **do**
4. <span class="algorithm-indent"></span>$\operatorname{Backward}(\ell,\, \theta_t^{(\ell)})$<span class="algorithm-comment">▹ per-sample $\{\partial \ell / \partial e_i^{(\ell)}\} \cup \{\partial \ell / \partial e_j^{\star(\ell)}\}$</span>
5. <span class="algorithm-indent"></span>$g_i^{(\ell)} \leftarrow \sum_{\tau} (\partial \ell / \partial e_{i,\tau}^{(\ell)}) \otimes a_{i,\tau}^{(\ell)}$<span class="algorithm-comment">▹ or PIP / compressed</span>
6. <span class="algorithm-indent"></span>$\hat g_\star^{(\ell)} \leftarrow \dfrac{1}{m}\sum_{j,\tau} (\partial \ell / \partial e_{j,\tau}^{\star(\ell)}) \otimes a_{j,\tau}^{\star(\ell)}$
7. <span class="algorithm-indent"></span>$s_i^{(\ell)} \leftarrow \langle \hat g_\star^{(\ell)},\; g_i^{(\ell)} \rangle$
8. <span class="algorithm-indent"></span>$S_{t,\ell} \leftarrow \operatorname{Top\text{-}k}(\{s_i^{(\ell)}\}_{i=1}^n,\, k)$
9. <span class="algorithm-indent"></span>$u_t^{(\ell)} \leftarrow \dfrac{1}{k}\sum_{i \in S_{t,\ell}} g_i^{(\ell)}$
10. <span class="algorithm-indent"></span>$\operatorname{Release}(a_i^{(\ell)},\; \partial \ell / \partial e_i^{(\ell)},\; a_j^{\star(\ell)},\; \partial \ell / \partial e_j^{\star(\ell)})$
11. **end for**
12. $\theta_{t+1} \leftarrow \theta_t - \eta_t u_t$

</div>

The schedule composes cleanly with **LoRA** (the outer-product factorization carries over to adapters), **MeSO** (the same compressed gradients drive both scoring and optimizer state), and **activation checkpointing** whenever the partition aligns with the checkpoint segments — Layer-Wise Subset satisfies this trivially.

## Results

We evaluate on Llama-3.2-1B (SFT), GPT-Neo-2.7B with LoRA (RLHF), and Qwen3-1.7B (RLVR), comparing against standard training (**Full-Training**), prior state-of-the-art curation ([**GREATS**](https://openreview.net/forum?id=232VcN8tSx) for SFT, [**IIF**](https://openreview.net/forum?id=sYK4yPDuT1) for RL; both correspond to our **Global Subset** in this framework), and our **Layer-Wise Subset Update**. All numbers are averaged over 5 seeds.

### SFT: Layer-Wise Subset wins on every QA setting

With a held-out target set of just 16 samples (per the GREATS protocol; $n = 8$, $m = 1$, $k = n/2$), Layer-Wise Subset Update consistently and substantially beats Full-Training and Global Subset.

### RLHF: best reward, lowest toxicity, across every ablation

The RLHF setting is more delicate than SFT because the *target objective* itself has two design degrees of freedom that interact non-trivially with data regularization. We detoxify GPT-Neo-2.7B with TRL+PPO and ablate both:

- **Target-signal source.** *Self-reference* — use the same batch as both training and target signal, with a soft preference inducted by the reward. *Held-out target* — reserve a separate target set drawn from the desired distribution.
- **Target loss form.** *Avg-reward* — reward-weighted log-probability of the target batch. *Pre-clip-PPO* — the PPO surrogate before clipping (a more aggressive policy-gradient signal).

This gives a 2×2 configuration grid. Against standard PPO, IIF, and Global Subset, **Layer-Wise Subset Update achieves the highest training reward and the lowest evaluation toxicity in every one of the four configurations**, with Global Subset second across the board.

Three patterns are worth calling out:

- **Self-reference accelerates early convergence; held-out reaches a higher final reward.** Self-reference uses the same on-policy batch as both training and target, so the target signal is dense and cheap from step one. The held-out target imposes a stricter alignment constraint per step but better matches the deployment distribution, so the long-run reward is higher and toxicity is lower.
- **The avg-reward objective dominates the pre-clip PPO surrogate** as the target loss. PPO's clipped surrogate was designed to stabilize *policy updates*, not to serve as a *target signal* for selecting training samples — using it in that role caps the reward achievable across all methods.
- **Layer-Wise is the clear winner in every configuration.** The same per-layer story as in SFT applies: PPO gradients are concentrated in a small fraction of linear layers, so a global subset picked by aggregate scores misallocates compute. Per-layer selection lets every module see samples that actually align with its own gradient direction.

### System efficiency: overhead vanishes with scale

**Takeaway.** The leverage is the *primal–dual* view of **data selection ↔ data regularization**. Jumping between the two reinterprets existing methods on a single bias–variance spectrum and suggests new ones the primal view never would — often with surprising findings (e.g., the 32× per-layer score-magnitude gap that lets Layer-Wise beat Global Subset).
