**TL;DR.** **dattri** is a PyTorch library that unifies data attribution: a single `AttributionTask` / `Attributor` API across the four main efficient method families (IF, TracIn, RPS, TRAK), reusable low-level primitives (HVP, IHVP, random projection, dropout ensembles) on top of `torch.func`, and a benchmark suite with pre-trained checkpoints and ground-truth annotations spanning image classification, music generation, and text generation. Used as a controlled head-to-head, the benchmark shows IF wins on linear/convex settings, TRAK with ensembling wins almost everywhere else, and the standard LOO metric collapses under non-convexity — LDS is the more faithful signal.

## Why dattri

- **One API, four method families.** A user swaps between Influence Function (Explicit, CG, LiSSA, Arnoldi), TracIn (TracInCP, Grad-Dot, Grad-Cos), RPS-L2, and TRAK (1/10/50-model ensembles) by changing the `Attributor` class — the surrounding PyTorch pipeline is untouched.
- **Low-level utilities, not just a wrapper.** HVP/IHVP, random projection (via `fast_jl`), and dropout-ensembling are exposed as composable primitives on `torch.func`. Researchers building new methods reuse them — the appendix shows a 5-line swap from random projection to Arnoldi projection inside TRAK.
- **First benchmark to cover generative settings.** Six settings — LR/MLP on MNIST-10, ResNet-9 on CIFAR-2/10, Music Transformer on MAESTRO (13.3M params), NanoGPT on Shakespeare (10.7M params) — graded on LOO, LDS, and noisy-label AUC. Generative AI tasks were absent from prior libraries.
- **Pre-trained checkpoints shipped.** 5000 exact-LOO models per MNIST setting and 100 half-dataset models per setting for LDS — costing roughly 500 A40-hours to produce — bypassing the retraining tax that has gated every previous fair comparison.
- **Concrete empirical findings.** TRAK-50 attains $0.37$ LDS on ResNet-9/CIFAR-2 where every IF variant OOMs, $0.32$ on MAESTRO and $0.23$ on Shakespeare where they remain infeasible; meanwhile the LOO ground truth on MLP collapses to $\leq 0.03$ for *every* method, while LDS still separates them — a sharp argument for retiring LOO as the headline metric.

## Method: library architecture

### Unified API

A user defines a callable loss $f(\theta, z)$ that operates on `functional_call(model, params, x)` and wraps it in an `AttributionTask`. Each attribution method is a class. Switching methods is a one-line change:

```python
attr_task = AttributionTask(
    loss_func=f, model=model, checkpoints=model.state_dict(),
    target_func=f,  # may differ from loss, e.g. the prediction logit
)
attributor = IFAttributorCG(task=attr_task, device=torch.device("cuda"),
                            **attributor_hyperparams)
attributor.cache(train_loader)
score = attributor.attribute(train_loader, test_loader)
```

The `cache` step pre-computes anything depending only on $(x, \Theta)$ — e.g. an IHVP factor — so that repeated $(x, \Theta, v)$ queries with varying $v$ amortize cleanly.

### Modular utilities

The `dattri.func` submodule, built on `torch.func`, exposes the mathematical primitives shared across attribution methods:

- **HVP / IHVP.** Given $H(x;\Theta) = \nabla_\Theta^2 f_\Theta(x)$, $\operatorname{HVP}(x,v;\Theta) = H(x;\Theta)\,v$ is a thin wrapper on composed JVPs. The IHVP $H^{-1}v$ admits four approximations — Explicit, CG, LiSSA, and Arnoldi — each provided in two flavors: `ihvp_{alg}(f)` returns a function of $(x,v)$, while `ihvp_at_x_{alg}(f, x, \Theta)` caches the $(x,\Theta)$-dependent work and returns a function of $v$ only.
- **Random projection.** A wrapper on `fast_jl` ([Park et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14186)) for the per-sample gradient sketch used by TRAK and (optionally) TracIn — the only way to make $g^\top g'$ over millions of parameters tractable at scale.
- **Dropout ensemble.** `dattri.model_utils.activate_dropout` realizes the [Deng & Ma (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.17293) trick: instead of training $k$ independent models for ensembling, take $k$ stochastic forward passes through a single model with dropout active, recovering most of the ensemble gain at $1/k$ the training cost.

Six controlled settings spanning $7.8\mathrm{k}$ to $13.3\mathrm{M}$ parameters:

Three evaluation metrics are bundled. **LOO correlation** measures Pearson $\rho$ between predicted and exact leave-one-out output deltas, requiring $n$ retrained models. **LDS** ([Ilyas et al., 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.00622)) treats attribution as a counterfactual predictor: given $m{=}50$ models trained on random half-subsets $\mathcal{S}_j$ at $\alpha{=}0.5$, it computes Spearman correlation between $\{f_{\Theta_{\mathcal{S}_j}}(x)\}_j$ and the additive proxy $g_\tau(x,\mathcal{S}_j;\mathcal{S}) := \sum_{i: x_i \in \mathcal{S}_j} \tau(x,\mathcal{S};f_\Theta)_i$. **AUC** scores ranking by self-influence $\tau_i := \tau(x_i, \{x_i\}; f_\Theta)$ for noisy-label detection.

### LDS vs LOO: the headline finding

The MLP/MNIST LOO column is uniformly $\leq 0.03$ for *every* method — including IF-CG and IF-LiSSA that score $0.26$ and $0.14$ on the same setting's LDS. The contrast is even sharper at scale: on ResNet-9/CIFAR-2, every IF variant OOMs and only TRAK ensembles deliver meaningful LDS ($0.10 \to 0.26 \to 0.37$ for TRAK-1/10/50). The same pattern holds on the generative settings, where TracIn, Grad-Dot, and TRAK are the only methods that even run, and TRAK-50 wins by $2\text{–}3\times$ in LDS.

Two takeaways the benchmark forces:

- **LOO is not a faithful ground truth in non-convex regimes.** This empirically corroborates [Bae et al. (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.05364): once the loss landscape has multiple minima, removing one training point may move the optimizer between basins, and the LOO output difference no longer reflects local influence. The LR setting ($0.80$ LDS) is the only one where LOO and LDS agree.
- **Ensembling is the deciding axis at scale.** A single-model TRAK-1 underperforms TracIn on MAESTRO ($0.07$ vs $0.13$ LDS), but TRAK-50 leads by $0.19$. The same story plays out on Shakespeare ($0.04 \to 0.23$). The library's $50$ shipped checkpoints per setting were specifically sized to make this comparison reproducible.

### Cost profile

Wall-clock and peak memory on a single A40 GPU for the two largest feasible settings:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Method | MNIST/MLP runtime | MNIST/MLP peak | CIFAR-2/ResNet-9 runtime | CIFAR-2/ResNet-9 peak |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| IF-Explicit | OOM | OOM | OOM | OOM |
| IF-CG | 611 s | 967 MB | OOM | OOM |
| IF-LiSSA | 499 s | 324 MB | OOM | OOM |
| IF-Arnoldi | 456 s | 251 MB | 733 s | 16.4 GB |
| TracInCP-10 | 86 s | 214 MB | 2250 s | 9.5 GB |
| Grad-Dot | 9 s | 214 MB | 227 s | 9.5 GB |
| RPS-L2 | 1.1 s | 72 MB | 1.2 s | 297 MB |
| TRAK-1 | 1.8 s | 207 MB | 37 s | 7.0 GB |
| TRAK-50 | 68 s | 2.2 GB | 1549 s | 9.1 GB |

</div>

TRAK's memory grows mostly with the projection dimension (constant in the ensemble size), and runtime scales linearly with the number of ensemble checkpoints. RPS-L2 is the cheapest by an order of magnitude because it only touches the last linear layer — useful as a fast sanity baseline, but the AUC and LDS columns show why it isn't a substitute for the gradient-based methods.
