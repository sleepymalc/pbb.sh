**TL;DR.** Data-attribution-based compensation is *not* adversarially robust. A malicious data provider who controls $\sim 1\%$ of the round-$t{=}1$ corpus can inflate their share of top-$k$ influential points by **2–7$\times$** with imperceptible feature perturbations — either by training shadow models on the public distribution (**Shadow Attack**, gray-box) or by turning their own samples into adversarial examples (**Outlier Attack**, black-box).

## Why Adversarial Attack on Data Attribution

- **First systematic threat model.** Periodic data contribution gives the adversary persistence ($Z_0 \subseteq Z_1^b$ with $\lvert Z_0 \rvert / \lvert Z_1^b \rvert \to 1$); the adversary controls $Z_1^a$ only and is paid by *compensation share* — the fraction of validation points for which their data lands in the top-$k$.
- **Two attacks at two capability levels.** *Shadow Attack* exploits a known data distribution and training algorithm (Assumption: distribution access). *Outlier Attack* exploits only black-box query access (Assumption: prediction queries) and a structural inductive bias of attribution methods.
- **Outlier $\Rightarrow$ influence, formally.** Theorem 5.1 shows that for any $\Theta(1)$-bounded influence function, perturbing a single training point changes *all other* influence scores by only $O(1/n)$ — so adversarial-example gradient inflation on the original model transfers to the retrained model.
- **Large, reproducible inflation.** Shadow Attack inflates compensation by **206–456%** across MNIST/Digits/CIFAR-10; Outlier Attack reaches **185–644%** and extends to text generation (NanoGPT on Shakespeare, 263%). Matched-budget random perturbation moves the needle by $<5\%$.

## Method

### Threat model

A model is retrained each round on the accumulated corpus and the top-$k$ most influential training points per validation example are compensated. Consider two consecutive rounds $t \in \{0, 1\}$. At $t=0$ only benign providers contribute $Z_0$; at $t=1$ the corpus is $Z_1 = Z_1^b \cup Z_1^a$ with $Z_1^a$ supplied by an adversary. Let $\tau_1 = \mathcal{A}(Z_1, \mathcal{T}(Z_1), V_1)$ be the resulting contribution function. The adversary maximizes the *compensation share*

$$
c(Z_1^a) \;=\; \frac{1}{k \lvert V_1 \rvert} \sum_{z \in Z_1^a} \sum_{v \in V_1} \mathbf{1}\!\left[\tau_1(z, v) \in \operatorname{Top}_k\!\big(\{\tau_1(z', v) : z' \in Z_1\}\big)\right].
$$

The adversary does **not** have white-box access to $\mathcal{T}(Z_0), \mathcal{T}(Z_1)$, the exact $Z_0, Z_1^b, V_0, V_1$, or the contribution functions; they may only perturb a small set of real data points (no synthetic samples). The two attacks live on opposite ends of the capability spectrum:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Attack | Knowledge required | Mechanism | Cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Shadow Attack** | data distribution + training algorithm $\mathcal{T}$ | gradient ascent on a surrogate attribution over $m$ shadow models | $m \times$ (train + score) |
| **Outlier Attack** | black-box prediction queries to $\mathcal{T}(Z_0)$ | off-the-shelf adversarial-example generation against $\mathcal{T}(Z_0)$ | one black-box attack per point |

</div>

### Shadow Attack: shadow training as a proxy oracle

The adversary samples $m$ shadow training sets $Z^{(1)}, \dots, Z^{(m)}$ and a shadow validation set $V^{(0)}$ from the assumed distribution, trains shadow models $M^{(i)} = \mathcal{T}(Z^{(i)} \cup Z)$, and computes shadow contribution functions $\tau^{(i)} = \mathcal{A}(Z^{(i)} \cup Z, M^{(i)}, V^{(0)})$. The natural objective $c_s(Z) = \frac{1}{m k \lvert V^{(0)} \rvert} \sum_{i,z,v} \mathbf{1}[\tau^{(i)}(z, v) \in \operatorname{Top}_k(\cdot)]$ is discrete and requires retraining each $M^{(i)}$ at every gradient step. Two simplifications make the attack tractable:

- **Continuous surrogate.** Replace the top-$k$ indicator with the raw attribution sum
  $$
  c'_s(Z) \;=\; \frac{1}{m k \lvert V^{(0)} \rvert} \sum_{i=1}^m \sum_{z \in Z} \sum_{v \in V^{(0)}} \tau^{(i)}(z, v).
  $$
- **Cheap attribution under fixed weights.** Freeze the shadow weights at their pre-perturbation values and instantiate $\tau^{(i)}$ as **Grad-Dot**, $\tau^{(i)}(z, v) = \langle \nabla_\theta \ell(M^{(i)}, z),\, \nabla_\theta \ell(M^{(i)}, v) \rangle$. Each gradient-ascent step then costs one forward–backward pass per shadow model, with **no retraining**. Empirically this surrogate transfers to *advanced* attribution methods (Influence Function, TRAK, Data Shapley) used by the actual AI developer.

Perturbations are projected back into the budget set $\mathcal{N}(Z)$ after each step; the procedure converges to a local optimum of $c'_s$ under a bounded budget.

### Outlier Attack: turning samples into outliers in the black box

Many attribution methods inherit an *outlier-inflates-influence* inductive bias — historically the original use case of the influence function was outlier detection ([Cook, 1977](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1268249)). The Outlier Attack converts each adversary-owned point $z = (x, y)$ into a model-specific outlier by perturbing $x$ alone (never the label $y$) to maximize the loss $\ell(\mathcal{T}(Z_0), (x, y))$ using off-the-shelf *black-box* adversarial-example methods:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Setting | Black-box attack | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Small-scale image | **ZOO** ([Chen et al., 2017](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3128572.3140448)) | finite-difference gradient estimate via prediction queries |
| Large-scale image | **SimBA** ([Guo et al., 2019](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v97/guo19a.html)) | coordinate descent, $O(d)$ queries |
| Text generation | **TextFooler** ([Jin et al., 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/1907.11932)) | synonym substitution under semantic constraints |

</div>

Keeping the label fixed is essential: a flipped $y$ would be a *negatively* influential mislabel, and quality filters tend to catch label noise. Perturbing $x$ only yields a sample that is statistically anomalous to the model but stays on the data manifold.

#### Why the perturbation transfers to the retrained model

The attack inflates gradients on $\hat\theta = \mathcal{T}(Z_0)$, but the *paid* attribution $\tau_{\mathrm{IF}}(z_j', z_{\text{test}}; \hat\theta')$ is computed on the retrained model $\hat\theta' = \mathcal{T}(Z \setminus \{z_j\} \cup \{z_j'\})$. The following theorem closes that gap.

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem 5.1** (Informal). *Consider ERM with twice-differentiable, $m$-strongly convex, $L$-Lipschitz loss $\ell$, and influence-function attribution*

$$
\tau_{\mathrm{IF}}(z_j, z_{\text{test}}; \hat\theta) \;=\; -\nabla_\theta \ell(\hat\theta, z_{\text{test}})^{\!\top} H(\hat\theta)^{-1} \nabla_\theta \ell(\hat\theta, z_j),
\qquad H(\hat\theta) \coloneqq \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \nabla_\theta^2 \ell(\hat\theta, z_i),
$$

*with $\lvert \tau_{\mathrm{IF}} \rvert, \lVert \nabla_\theta \ell \rVert_2, \lVert \nabla_\theta^2 \ell \rVert_{\mathrm{op}} = \Theta(1)$. If $z_j \to z_j'$, then for every test point $z_{\text{test}}$ and every $i \ne j$,*

$$
\tau_{\mathrm{IF}}(z_i, z_{\text{test}}; \hat\theta') \;=\; \tau_{\mathrm{IF}}(z_i, z_{\text{test}}; \hat\theta) + O(1/n),
\qquad
\tau_{\mathrm{IF}}(z_j', z_{\text{test}}; \hat\theta') \;=\; \tau_{\mathrm{IF}}(z_j', z_{\text{test}}; \hat\theta) + O(1/n).
$$

</div>

The proof goes through $\lVert \hat\theta' - \hat\theta \rVert \le 4L / (mn)$ (strong convexity + Lipschitzness) and a first-order Neumann expansion $\lVert H'^{-1} - H^{-1} \rVert_{\mathrm{op}} = O(4LM / (m^3 n))$, then chains them through the influence formula. Generalizing to $k$ simultaneous perturbations sharpens the bound to $O(k/n)$.

<div class="takeaway">

**Why this matters for the attack.** The adversary can score perturbations on $\hat\theta$ (cheap, no retraining) while the platform pays based on $\hat\theta'$ (expensive, post-retraining). Theorem 5.1 says the two scores are within $O(1/n)$, so adversarial-example gradient inflation on $\hat\theta$ — which is exactly what the black-box attacks optimize — survives the retrain.

</div>

## Results

We measure the **Ratio** = $c(Z_1^a)_{\text{manipulated}} / c(Z_1^a)_{\text{original}}$ and the **More / Tied / Fewer** breakdown over $V_1$ at $k = 100$. Settings (a)–(d) are image classification: (a) MNIST/LR with Influence Function, (b) Digits/MLP with Data Shapley, (c) MNIST/CNN with TRAK, (d) CIFAR-10/ResNet-18 with TRAK. Setting (e) is text generation: Shakespeare/NanoGPT with TRAK. In all image settings $\lvert Z_1^a \rvert / \lvert Z_1 \rvert \approx 1\%$ (Digits: $2.5\%$ / $3.5\%$); for text $\lvert Z_1^a \rvert / \lvert Z_1 \rvert \approx 0.3\%$.

### Shadow Attack

<div class="table-scroll">

| Setting | Shadow model | Attribution | Original $\to$ Manipulated | **Ratio** | More / Tied / Fewer |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| (a) MNIST | LR | Influence Function | $0.0098 \to 0.0477$ | **456.1%** | 0.955 / 0.038 / 0.007 |
| (b) Digits | MLP | Data Shapley | $0.0152 \to 0.0435$ | 286.2% | 0.533 / 0.333 / 0.134 |
| (c) MNIST | CNN | TRAK | $0.0112 \to 0.0467$ | 417.0% | 0.781 / 0.195 / 0.024 |
| (d) CIFAR-10 | ResNet-18 | TRAK | $0.0095 \to 0.0213$ | 217.3% | 0.655 / 0.259 / 0.086 |
| (d) CIFAR-10 | **ResNet-9** (mismatch) | TRAK | $0.0095 \to 0.0196$ | 206.3% | 0.622 / 0.310 / 0.068 |

</div>

*Ratios across the board sit between $206\%$ and $456\%$, with the gain concentrated in **More** (the attack helps a strict majority of validation points). The last row uses a smaller shadow architecture (ResNet-9) than the target (ResNet-18): the attack still produces a $2\times$ inflation, so exact architectural knowledge is **not** required.*

### Outlier Attack

<div class="table-scroll">

| Setting | Black-box attack | Attribution | Original $\to$ Manipulated | **Ratio** | More / Tied / Fewer |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| (a) MNIST | ZOO | Influence Function | $0.0098 \to 0.0631$ | **643.9%** | 0.980 / 0.017 / 0.003 |
| (b) Digits | SimBA | Data Shapley | $0.0112 \to 0.0218$ | 194.6% | 0.440 / 0.380 / 0.180 |
| (c) MNIST | SimBA | TRAK | $0.0112 \to 0.0668$ | 596.4% | 0.799 / 0.173 / 0.028 |
| (d) CIFAR-10 | SimBA | TRAK | $0.0095 \to 0.0176$ | 185.2% | 0.562 / 0.354 / 0.084 |
| (e) Shakespeare/NanoGPT | TextFooler | TRAK | $0.0035 \to 0.0092$ | 262.9% | 0.392 / 0.461 / 0.147 |

</div>

*Outlier Attack matches or exceeds Shadow Attack on (a) and (c) — despite needing **only black-box queries** — and is the only attack that ports to generative-AI settings ((e), no usable distributional assumption). The ResNet-18 result on (d) is the hardest setting for both attacks: high-capacity models concentrate influence on fewer, more idiosyncratic samples, and our $1\%$ adversary budget still doubles their compensation.*

### Random-perturbation control

To rule out a "any perturbation inflates influence" explanation, we run pixel-wise random perturbation with the **same $\ell_\infty$ budget** as the proposed attacks:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Setting | Original | Random Perturbation | More / Tied / Fewer |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| (a) MNIST | 0.0098 | 0.0097 | 0.203 / 0.586 / 0.211 |
| (c) MNIST | 0.0112 | 0.0117 | 0.385 / 0.326 / 0.289 |
| (d) CIFAR-10 | 0.0095 | 0.0125 | 0.367 / 0.430 / 0.203 |

</div>

The compensation share moves by **at most $\sim 4\%$ absolute** under random noise and **More $\approx$ Fewer** — i.e., the inflation in Shadow / Outlier comes from the *structured* perturbation, not from injecting any high-frequency noise into the training corpus.

<div class="takeaway">

**Takeaway.** Data-attribution-based compensation is *not* adversarially robust by default. The very inductive bias that makes attribution useful — outliers inflate influence — makes it gameable by an adversary controlling $\sim 1\%$ of the corpus, with **black-box queries alone**. Any production data economy needs adversarial robustness baked into the attribution method, not bolted on as a downstream filter.

</div>
