## Problem

The classic [*influence function*](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.04730) answers a narrow but well-studied question: how much does removing a *single* training sample shift a model's predictions? In practice, the question that matters is broader — which *group* of samples is collectively responsible for a model's behavior? Identifying the most harmful or most informative subset drives applications from data debugging to dataset pruning, yet the single-sample tool does not straightforwardly extend. The dominant approach applies influence-based greedy heuristics: score every sample by its individual influence, pick the top scorer, and repeat. This one-pass procedure implicitly treats collective influence as the sum of individual scores — an assumption that can be dramatically wrong.

## Key Idea

We formalize this as the **Most Influential Subset Selection (MISS)** problem: given training data and a target metric, find the subset $S$ of size at most $k$ whose removal causes the largest change in that metric. We then ask whether standard influence-based greedy heuristics reliably solve it. The answer is no — and provably so. Even in linear regression, these heuristics can select entirely wrong subsets, making MISS a strictly harder problem than its single-sample counterpart.

## Approach

The analysis isolates two independent failure modes. The first is *influence-function approximation error*: influence scores systematically underestimate the effect of high-leverage samples, because they omit the self-influence correction factor $1/(1 - h_{ii})$ that scales with a sample's leverage score $h_{ii}$. A greedy procedure built on these scores therefore misorients itself from the very first step.

The second failure mode is *non-additivity of collective influence*. Even with perfect individual scores, greedy selection can fail because group effects do not decompose additively. Two distinct failure patterns arise: *amplification*, where a cluster of high-leverage samples has a collective impact that grows super-linearly and far exceeds the sum of individual effects; and *cancellation*, where cross-leverage between samples causes their joint effect to fall *below* the sum, so the greedy pair is not the optimal pair.

To address both modes, we propose an *adaptive iterative* variant of the greedy heuristic. Rather than computing all influence scores once against the full training set, it refits the model and recomputes scores at each selection step, so that each new score reflects the marginal contribution of a candidate sample given those already selected. This directly accounts for sample interactions. Under the cancellation setup, we prove that adaptive greedy recovers the optimal 2-MISS solution when the most influential sample belongs to the optimal subset — a guarantee the static heuristic lacks. Adaptivity comes at the cost of $k$ model refits, making the performance–computation tradeoff explicit.

## Result

Experiments across four real-world datasets — spanning linear regression (Concrete Compressive Strength), logistic regression (Waveform), and a non-linear MLP (MNIST) — corroborate the theoretical picture. The adaptive heuristic achieves a consistently higher averaged actual effect $\bar{A}_{-S}$ and wins on the majority of test instances across all values of $k$. Crucially, the advantage extends beyond the linear setting in which the theory is derived, suggesting that non-additivity is a pervasive rather than pathological phenomenon. The results frame adaptivity as a principled, if computationally heavier, default for MISS, and surface the performance–computation tradeoff as the central open question for future work.
