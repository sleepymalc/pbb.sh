**TL;DR.** **MISS** asks for the size-$k$ training subset whose removal *most* changes a target metric. The standard recipe — score every sample once, take the top-$k$ — provably fails even in OLS: it forgets the leverage correction $1/(1-h_{ii})$ in individual scores, and it forgets that collective influence is *not* additive (clusters *amplify*, cross-leverage *cancels*). An *adaptive iterative* greedy — refit, rescore, repeat — recovers the optimal $2$-MISS solution under cancellation and wins across linear/logistic/MLP benchmarks at the cost of $k$ refits.

Influence functions answer a narrow single-sample question: how much does removing $(x_i, y_i)$ shift a chosen statistic? Real applications — data debugging, robustness audits, dataset pruning — instead ask for the *group* of samples collectively responsible. The dominant approach scores every sample by its individual influence, takes the top-$k$, and implicitly assumes group effects are the *sum* of individual ones. We show that assumption is wrong even for ordinary least squares, characterize *why* it is wrong, and pin down when refitting between selections fixes it.

## Why MISS

- **Collective $\neq$ sum of individuals.** Even with perfect single-sample scores, greedy can pick the wrong pair in OLS. The non-additivity has two faces: *amplification* (cluster of high-leverage samples blows past $\sum_i A_{-\{i\}}$) and *cancellation* (cross-leverage drags the pair *below* the sum).
- **Influence scores under-estimate high-leverage samples by exactly $1/(1-h_{ii})$.** This factor is missing from ZAMinfluence — so a sample whose leverage is large enough to flip $1$-MISS is precisely the one the score under-ranks.
- **Adaptive greedy provably recovers $2$-MISS under cancellation** (Theorem). The refit-and-rescore variant reads marginal contribution given previous picks, paying $k$ refits for a guarantee the static heuristic lacks.
- **Holds beyond linear theory.** Adaptivity wins on the averaged actual effect $\overline{A_{-S}}$ across *all* tested $k$ on Concrete (OLS), Waveform (logistic), and MNIST (MLP) — the failure modes are pervasive, not pathological.

## Method

### Problem formulation

Let $\hat\theta = \arg\min_\theta \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n L(f(\theta, x_i), y_i)$ and, for any $S \subseteq [n]$, let $\hat\theta_{-S}$ minimize the same objective with $S$ dropped. Given a target function $\phi:\mathbb{R}^q\to\mathbb{R}$ (e.g. a test prediction), the *actual effect* of removing $S$ is

$$
A_{-S} \;\coloneqq\; \phi(\hat\theta_{-S}) - \phi(\hat\theta).
$$

For $k \ll n$, **$k$-MISS** seeks $S_{\mathrm{opt},k} = \arg\max_{|S|\leq k} A_{-S}$. It generalizes single-sample influence ($k=1$) to a *worst-case collective influence*. The decision version is NP-hard (a variant subsumes robust regression), and naive enumeration is $\binom{n}{k}$ — hence the influence-greedy heuristics.

We work in OLS for theory: $X\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$, $y=X\theta^\star - e$, $N = X^\top X$, hat matrix $H = X N^{-1} X^\top$ with diagonal *leverage* $h_{ii}$ and off-diagonal *cross-leverage* $h_{ij}$, residual $r_i = x_i^\top \hat\theta - y_i$, and target $\phi(\theta) = x_{\mathrm{test}}^\top \theta$.

### Failure mode 1: leverage-blind influence scores

<figure class="figure-block figure-right">
  <img src="/research/MISS/Figures/IF.png" alt="OLS regression on an 8-point synthetic dataset: removing the high-leverage point 1 (green line) tilts the regression most at the test location, but the influence-function score ranks point 8 (pink line) higher. The legend lists scaled influence estimates by sample." />
  <figcaption><strong>Influence ranks the wrong sample.</strong> ZAMinfluence's top-scored point (<span style="color:#e879ad">8</span>) is <em>not</em> the one whose removal moves the test prediction the most — that is the high-leverage point (<span style="color:#16a34a">1</span>). The miss is exactly the $1/(1-h_{ii})$ correction the influence function drops.</figcaption>
</figure>

Plugging squared loss into the influence-function definition, ZAMinfluence scores each sample by $v_i = x_{\mathrm{test}}^\top N^{-1} x_i r_i$. The classical leave-one-out identity from regression diagnostics gives the *exact* perturbation,

$$
\hat\theta_{-\{i\}} - \hat\theta \;=\; \frac{N^{-1} x_i r_i}{1 - h_{ii}}, \qquad A_{-\{i\}} \;=\; \frac{x_{\mathrm{test}}^\top N^{-1} x_i r_i}{1 - h_{ii}} \;=\; \frac{v_i}{1 - h_{ii}}.
$$

So the influence score *systematically under-estimates* the individual effect by exactly the self-influence factor

$$
\boxed{\;\frac{1}{1 - h_{ii}}\;}
$$

The error scales with leverage, so the under-estimate is largest on the samples most likely to be the answer — outliers and high-leverage points.

<div class="table-scroll">

| Quantity | Under-estimates by | When is the gap large? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| $v_i = x_{\mathrm{test}}^\top N^{-1} x_i r_i$ (ZAMinfluence) | factor $(1 - h_{ii})$ | $h_{ii} \to 1$ (high-leverage / outlier $x_i$) |
| $A_{-\{i\}} = v_i / (1 - h_{ii})$ (LAGS, exact) | $-$ | $-$ |

</div>

A simple correction yields **Leverage-Adjusted Greedy Selection (LAGS)**: score by $A_{-\{i\}}$ instead of $v_i$. We prove (Thm. 1) that whenever $h_{11} > h_{nn}$, there exists a label-generation scale $p$ on which ZAMinfluence picks the wrong $1$-MISS sample — LAGS does not.

### Failure mode 2: non-additivity of collective influence

    <figcaption><strong>Amplification.</strong> Points <span style="color:#e879ad">7</span>, <span style="color:#e879ad">8</span> have the top individual effects, yet the green high-leverage cluster $\{1, 2\}$ collectively swings the test prediction further: $A_{-\{1,2\}} > A_{-\{7,8\}}$. LAGS picks $\{7,8\}$ and loses.</figcaption>
  </figure>
  <figure class="figure-block">

    <figcaption><strong>Cancellation.</strong> Points <span style="color:#e879ad">8</span> and <span style="color:#16a34a">1</span> have the top-$2$ individual effects, but their cross-leverage flips signs: $A_{-\{1,8\}} < A_{-\{8\}}$ — removing both <em>together</em> is worse than removing <span style="color:#e879ad">8</span> alone. LAGS picks the cancelling pair.</figcaption>
  </figure>
</div>

LAGS fixes the per-sample score but still aggregates additively. Closed-form pair analysis exposes why this is wrong. For $S = \{i, j\}$ in OLS,

$$
A_{-\{i,j\}}
\;=\;
\frac{(1 - h_{ii})(1 - h_{jj})\bigl(A_{-\{i\}} + A_{-\{j\}}\bigr) \;+\; h_{ij}\,x_{\mathrm{test}}^\top N^{-1}(x_i r_j + x_j r_i)}
     {(1 - h_{ii})(1 - h_{jj}) - h_{ij}^2}.
$$

Two cross-terms drive non-additivity: $h_{ij}^2$ in the denominator *inflates* the sum, and $h_{ij}(x_i r_j + x_j r_i)$ in the numerator can *cancel* it. Two regimes follow:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Regime | Trigger | Pair effect vs. sum | LAGS verdict |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Amplification** | cluster of identical / high-leverage samples; $h_{ij}$ near $h_{ii}$ | $A_{-\{i,j\}} \gg A_{-\{i\}} + A_{-\{j\}}$ | misses cluster; picks high-residual pair |
| **Cancellation** | $\mathrm{sign}(h_{ij}) \neq \mathrm{sign}(r_n/r_1)$, small $\lvert A_{-\{1\}}\rvert$ | $A_{-\{i,j\}} < \max\bigl(A_{-\{i\}}, A_{-\{j\}}\bigr)$ | picks two singly-strong samples that *fight* each other |

</div>

For amplification, $c$ identical copies of $(x_i, y_i)$ give

$$
\frac{A_{-\{i\}^c}}{A_{-\{i\}}} \;=\; \frac{c\,(1 - h_{ii})}{1 - c\,h_{ii}} \;>\; c,
$$

unbounded as $h_{ii} \to 1/c$ (Prop. 4): even a minor-influence sample becomes catastrophic in cluster. Theorem 2 makes this concrete — for $c$ copies each of $(x_1,y_1),(x_n,y_n)$ with $h_{11} > h_{nn}$, there exists $p$ where LAGS fails $c$-MISS. Theorem 3 does the same for cancellation in $2$-MISS, requiring only a single non-zero cross-leverage $h_{1n}$.

<div class="takeaway">

**Takeaway.** LAGS works iff all cross-leverages are zero — a measure-zero condition. The first-order approximation drops the Neumann series $(I_k - X_S N^{-1} X_S^\top)^{-1} = I_k + M_S + M_S^2 + \cdots$ down to $I_k$; the failure modes are the omitted terms.

</div>

### Adaptive iterative greedy

The fix is structural, not algebraic. At step $t$ with current selection $S_t$:
1. **Refit** the model on $[n]\setminus S_t$, obtaining $\hat\theta_{-S_t}$.
2. **Rescore** every remaining $i$ by its individual effect *given* the refit:
   $A'_{-\{i\}} \;\coloneqq\; \phi(\hat\theta_{-S_t \cup \{i\}}) - \phi(\hat\theta_{-S_t})$.
3. **Append** $\arg\max_i A'_{-\{i\}}$ (if positive) to $S_t$; repeat until $|S_t|=k$.

The new score $A'$ captures *marginal contribution given prior picks*, exactly the dependency LAGS ignores. Two properties of $A'$ (Prop. 5) drive the analysis:

- **Sign consistency.** $A'_{-\{i\}}$ and $A_{-\{i,n\}} - A_{-\{i\}}$ have the same sign, so the second pick reads the right direction.
- **Order preservation.** $\{A'_{-\{i\}}\}$ is order-isomorphic to $\{A_{-\{i,n\}}\}$, so ranking by $A'$ is ranking by true joint effect.

These give the central recovery result.

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Cancellation $\Rightarrow$ adaptive recovers $2$-MISS). *Under the OLS label model with $A_{-\{1\}}, A_{-\{n\}} > 0$ and $A_{-\{1,n\}} < A_{-\{n\}}$ (cancellation), if $n \in S_{\mathrm{opt},2}$, then the adaptive greedy algorithm returns $S_{\mathrm{opt},2}$.*

</div>

The price is explicit: $k$ refits instead of one, with the static and adaptive variants sitting on a clean tradeoff axis.

<div class="table-scroll">

| Variant | Per-sample score | Aggregation | Refits | Provable in cancellation? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ZAMinfluence | $v_i$ | additive top-$k$ | $0$ | no (also fails $1$-MISS) |
| **LAGS** | $A_{-\{i\}} = v_i/(1-h_{ii})$ | additive top-$k$ | $0$ | no |
| **Adaptive greedy** | $A'_{-\{i\}}$ (post-refit) | iterative argmax | $k$ | yes (Theorem) |

</div>

<div class="remark">

**Remark** (Amplification breaks adaptive too). Under *amplification*, adaptivity *also* fails — the recovery proof requires $n \in S_{\mathrm{opt},k}$, and amplification places the optimal subset inside a cluster the first refit never enters. Second-order group influence $Q_{-S} = x_{\mathrm{test}}^\top N^{-1} X_S^\top (I_k + X_S N^{-1} X_S^\top)(X_S\hat\theta - y_S)$ — keeping the next Neumann term — is the natural lever there, at the cost of submodularity ($Q_{-S}$ is generally non-submodular, so even $1{-}1/e$ guarantees are off the table).

</div>

## Results

We compare *vanilla greedy* (LAGS for regression, ZAMinfluence for classification) against *adaptive greedy* across a regression, a classification, and a non-linear setting. The metric is the **averaged actual effect** $\overline{A_{-S}} = \mathbb{E}_{z}\bigl[A_{-S}(z)\bigr]$ over held-out test points, plus a per-instance **winning rate** between the two algorithms.

<div class="table-scroll">

| Task | Dataset | Model | Greedy uses |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Regression | Concrete Compressive Strength (UCI) | OLS | LAGS |
| Classification | Waveform Database (UCI) | Logistic regression | ZAMinfluence |
| Classification (non-linear) | MNIST | 2-layer MLP, $5$-model ensemble | ZAMinfluence + ensemble |

</div>

For the MLP, the optimum is not unique, so we follow [TRAK](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14186)-style ensembling: $5$ models trained from identical initialization with different seeds, and both the influence estimate and the actual effect are averaged over the ensemble. Refits scale as $k\times \text{(ensemble size)}$ — a worst-case-by-design cost.

### Adaptive greedy wins across $k$ and across datasets

Across all three settings and all $k$ tested, adaptive greedy delivers a *higher* averaged actual effect than the vanilla baseline and a *winning rate above $50\%$* on the held-out instances. The gap is consistent rather than dataset-specific:

<div class="table-scroll">

| Setting | $\overline{A_{-S}}$ ranking | Winning rate (adaptive vs. vanilla) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Concrete / OLS | Adaptive $>$ LAGS, every $k$ | adaptive wins majority, every $k$ |
| Waveform / logistic | Adaptive $>$ ZAMinfluence, every $k$ | adaptive wins majority, every $k$ |
| MNIST / MLP (ensemble) | Adaptive $>$ ZAMinfluence, every $k$ | adaptive wins majority, every $k$ |

</div>

Two consequences are worth flagging:

- **Non-additivity is not a linear-theory artifact.** The MLP gap establishes that the *qualitative* picture — interactions between training samples drive the joint effect — survives nonlinearity, even though the closed-form leverage decomposition does not.
- **Performance–compute is the central knob.** The vanilla heuristic is $O(n)$ scores then a sort; adaptive pays $k$ retrains. The empirical gap argues that for any MISS deployment where the *answer* (not the runtime) is load-bearing, the refit cost is well spent. Hybrid scoring functions that approximate $A'$ without retraining are an obvious open direction.

A final implication for evaluation: any metric that *grades a data-attribution method by its agreement with an additive proxy* — most prominently the [Linear Datamodeling Score](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v202/park23c/park23c.pdf) — inherits the bias documented here. The MISS perspective suggests evaluation metrics need to be *non-additive and contextual* to discriminate between attribution methods that capture sample interactions and those that merely score well against an additive yardstick.

<div class="takeaway">

**Takeaway.** Collective influence is *not* the sum of individual influences — cluster amplification and cross-leverage cancellation are the rule, not the exception. Static top-$k$ greedy reads the wrong direction; **adaptive refit-and-rescore** reads marginal contribution given previous picks, and provably recovers $2$-MISS under cancellation. The same lesson hits evaluation: any metric scored against an *additive* proxy is blind to exactly the methods that capture sample interactions.

</div>
