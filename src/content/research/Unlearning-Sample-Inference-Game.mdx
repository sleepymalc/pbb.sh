**TL;DR.** We recast machine-unlearning evaluation as a *cryptographic two-player game*. The induced **Unlearning Quality** $\mathcal{Q} = 1 - \sup_{\mathcal{A}} \operatorname{Adv}(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Ul})$ is the first unlearning metric that simultaneously *zero-grounds* gold-standard retraining ($\mathcal{Q} = 1$), admits a *provable lower bound* under $(\epsilon, \delta)$-certified removal, and is *practically computable* via a two-split **SWAP test**.

Machine unlearning — selectively removing the influence of specific training data from a deployed model — has matured rapidly, yet *evaluating* unlearning remains an open problem. The dominant paradigm equates removal quality with membership-inference-attack (MIA) success rate, but this proxy is fundamentally brittle: gold-standard retraining is not guaranteed to *minimize* MIA success, results swing with the choice of attack and the data split, and no unified theory exists. We propose an evaluation that fixes all three failure modes at once.

## Why this metric

- **MIA accuracy is mis-calibrated.** Approximate methods can score *better* than retraining on MIA AUC, even though retraining is the theoretically optimal removal procedure — the metric punishes the gold standard.
- **No grounding, no upper bound.** Existing scores (MIA AUC, Interclass Confusion) give neither a guarantee that retraining maxes out the scale nor any inequality relating the score to formal removal notions such as $(\epsilon, \delta)$-certified removal.
- **Sensitive to attack and split choice.** Different MIAs and different forget/test partitions give incomparable numbers; the metric inherits all the brittleness of its underlying attack.
- **A cryptographic remedy.** Treat the unlearned model as a primitive and the MIA as an adversary; measure *advantage* — the gap between two indistinguishable scenarios — instead of raw attack success. Symmetry of the construction cancels per-sample MIA bias, and the worst-case advantage is what we report as $\mathcal{Q}$.

## Method

### The unlearning sample inference game

A game $\mathcal{G} = (\operatorname{Ul}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{D}, \mathbb{P}_{\mathcal{D}}, \alpha)$ between a *challenger* $\operatorname{Ul}$ (the unlearning algorithm under evaluation) and an *adversary* $\mathcal{A}$ (an MIA). The dataset $\mathcal{D}$ is randomly partitioned into three disjoint sets

$$
\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{R} \;\cup\; \mathcal{F} \;\cup\; \mathcal{T},
\qquad \alpha = \frac{|\mathcal{F}|}{|\mathcal{R} \cup \mathcal{F}|}, \qquad |\mathcal{F}| = |\mathcal{T}|,
$$

a *retain* set, a *forget* set, and a *test* set. Let $\mathcal{S}_\alpha$ denote the collection of all such splits. The game proceeds in three phases.

- **Initialization.** Sample $s = (\mathcal{R}, \mathcal{F}, \mathcal{T}) \sim \mathcal{U}(\mathcal{S}_\alpha)$ and a secret bit $b \in \{0, 1\}$. Construct a random oracle $\mathcal{O}_s(b)$ that emits a sample drawn from $\mathcal{F}$ when $b = 0$ and from $\mathcal{T}$ when $b = 1$, with sampling weights given by a *sensitivity distribution* $\mathbb{P}_{\mathcal{D}}$ (uniform unless prior risk weighting is desired).
- **Challenger.** $\operatorname{Ul}$ trains a model on $\mathcal{R} \cup \mathcal{F}$ via $\operatorname{Learn}$, then unlearns $\mathcal{F}$, producing $m = \operatorname{Ul}(\operatorname{Learn}(\mathcal{R} \cup \mathcal{F}), \mathcal{F}) \sim \mathbb{P}_{\mathcal{M}}(\operatorname{Ul}, s)$.
- **Adversary.** $\mathcal{A}$ is given black-box access to $m$ and to $\mathcal{O}_s(b)$ — but is told *neither* $s$ nor $b$ — and must output a guess $\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{O}}(m) \in \{0, 1\}$ of $b$. Intuitively, if unlearning truly erased $\mathcal{F}$, the forget samples and never-seen test samples are indistinguishable through $m$, and $\mathcal{A}$ has nothing to exploit.

### Unlearning Quality $\mathcal{Q}$

The cryptographic *advantage* — the gap between $\mathcal{A}$'s acceptance rate in the two oracle worlds, averaged over splits — is

$$
\operatorname{Adv}(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Ul})
\;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}_\alpha|} \left\lvert\, \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}_\alpha} \Pr\!\left[\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{O}_s(0)}(m) = 1\right] \;-\; \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}_\alpha} \Pr\!\left[\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{O}_s(1)}(m) = 1\right] \,\right\rvert,
$$

where each probability is also over $m \sim \mathbb{P}_{\mathcal{M}}(\operatorname{Ul}, s)$. The **Unlearning Quality** is the worst-case complement

$$
\boxed{\;\mathcal{Q}(\operatorname{Ul}) \;\coloneqq\; 1 \;-\; \sup_{\mathcal{A}} \operatorname{Adv}(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Ul}) \;\in\; [0, 1],\;}
$$

with higher meaning better. Two structural features make $\mathcal{Q}$ behave correctly. First, averaging over all splits in $\mathcal{S}_\alpha$ is *symmetric*: every data point has equal probability of landing in $\mathcal{F}$ versus $\mathcal{T}$, so any per-sample MIA bias cancels between symmetric splits. Second, the supremum makes $\mathcal{Q}$ a worst-case guarantee — it cannot be gamed by picking a weak attacker.

### Two provable guarantees

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Zero Grounding). *For any adversary $\mathcal{A}$, $\operatorname{Adv}(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Retrain}) = 0$, hence $\mathcal{Q}(\operatorname{Retrain}) = 1$.*

</div>

Because $\operatorname{Retrain}$ trains on $\mathcal{R}$ alone, both $\mathcal{F}$ and $\mathcal{T}$ are unseen to the retrained model; the two oracle worlds are *information-theoretically* identical, regardless of $\mathcal{A}$. The gold standard achieves the top of the scale automatically.

<div class="theorem">

**Theorem** (Certified-Removal Lower Bound). *If $\operatorname{Ul}$ is $(\epsilon, \delta)$-certified removal, then for every adversary $\mathcal{A}$,*

$$
\operatorname{Adv}(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Ul}) \;\leq\; 2 \left( 1 - \frac{2 - 2\delta}{e^\epsilon + 1} \right)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\mathcal{Q}(\operatorname{Ul}) \;\geq\; \frac{4 - 4\delta}{e^\epsilon + 1} - 1.
$$

</div>

The bound is decreasing in $\epsilon$ — smaller privacy budgets force higher $\mathcal{Q}$ — and is *the first inequality* connecting an empirical unlearning metric to a formal removal notion. This is what makes $\mathcal{Q}$ falsifiable: a working metric *must* track $\epsilon$.

### SWAP test approximation

Computing $\mathcal{Q}$ exactly requires enumerating $|\mathcal{S}_\alpha|$ splits — exponential in $|\mathcal{D}|$. We propose a two-split estimator. Pick a random $s = (\mathcal{R}, \mathcal{F}, \mathcal{T}) \in \mathcal{S}_\alpha$, *swap* the forget and test sets to get $s' = (\mathcal{R}, \mathcal{T}, \mathcal{F})$, and average:

$$
\overline{\operatorname{Adv}}_{\{s, s'\}}(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Ul})
\;\coloneqq\; \frac{1}{2}\,\bigl\lvert \operatorname{Adv}_s(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Ul}) + \operatorname{Adv}_{s'}(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Ul}) \bigr\rvert.
$$

Symmetry is the only structural property the zero-grounding proof needs, and the swap pair $\{s, s'\}$ inherits it.

<div class="proposition">

**Proposition.** *For any $\mathcal{A}$ and any swap pair $s, s' \in \mathcal{S}_\alpha$, $\overline{\operatorname{Adv}}_{\{s, s'\}}(\mathcal{A}, \operatorname{Retrain}) = 0$.*

</div>

<div class="remark">

**Remark** (Why SWAP, not random pairs). For any two non-degenerate *random* splits $s_1, s_2$, there exists a deterministic adversary achieving $\overline{\operatorname{Adv}}_{\{s_1, s_2\}} = 1$ against *every* unlearning method, including retraining. Zero-grounding requires symmetry between forget and test sets — and only the SWAP pair $\{s, s'\}$ has it.

</div>

For the supremum, we approximate it by the best of several state-of-the-art weak MIAs (shadow-model, correctness, confidence, modified-entropy), each making independent decisions on single oracle queries. As MIAs improve, $\mathcal{Q}$ tightens automatically.

## Results

We evaluate on CIFAR-10 with ResNet, the standard image-classification unlearning benchmark. The lineup spans the gold standard $\operatorname{Retrain}$, a no-op baseline $\operatorname{None}$, and six approximate unlearners: $\operatorname{Fisher}$, $\operatorname{FtFinal}$, $\operatorname{RetrFinal}$, $\operatorname{NegGrad}$, $\operatorname{SalUN}$, and the state-of-the-art $\operatorname{SSD}$.

### Stable rankings across dataset size and unlearning portion $\alpha$

A useful metric should produce a *consistent ranking* as we vary the experimental knobs. Holding $\alpha = 0.1$ and sweeping the fraction $\eta$ of CIFAR-10 used, $\operatorname{SSD} > \operatorname{Fisher} > \operatorname{SalUN} > \operatorname{RetrFinal}$ stays put across every column (and $\operatorname{Retrain}$ pins to $\approx 1$):

<div class="table-scroll">

| $\operatorname{Ul}$ | $\eta = 0.1$ | $\eta = 0.4$ | $\eta = 0.8$ | $\eta = 1.0$ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $\operatorname{Retrain}$ | **0.999** | **0.997** | **0.993** | **0.993** |
| $\operatorname{SSD}$ | 0.975 | 0.939 | 0.929 | 0.928 |
| $\operatorname{Fisher}$ | 0.751 | 0.679 | 0.734 | 0.791 |
| $\operatorname{SalUN}$ | 0.476 | 0.617 | 0.689 | 0.748 |
| $\operatorname{RetrFinal}$ | 0.340 | 0.586 | 0.621 | 0.634 |
| $\operatorname{NegGrad}$ | 0.124 | 0.564 | 0.603 | 0.656 |

</div>

The same pattern holds when we instead sweep the unlearning portion $\alpha \in \{0.1, 0.25, 0.4, 0.67\}$ — relative rankings are essentially preserved while the gold standard sits at the top. *Small-scale evaluations reliably predict large-scale ones*, which is what a benchmarking metric is for.

### Validating the certified-removal bound via DP

Verifying the lower bound directly is hard because $(\epsilon, \delta)$-certified removers are scarce beyond convex models. We exploit the implication: any $(\epsilon, \delta)$-DP model is automatically $(\epsilon, \delta)$-certified-removed for every training point, even without applying an unlearner. We train ResNet under DP-SGD with $\delta = 10^{-5}$ and $\epsilon \in \{50, 150, 600, \infty\}$, then measure $\mathcal{Q}$:

<div class="table-scroll">

| $\operatorname{Ul}$ | $\epsilon = 50$ | $\epsilon = 150$ | $\epsilon = 600$ | $\epsilon = \infty$ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $\operatorname{None}$ | 0.972 | 0.960 | 0.932 | 0.587 |
| $\operatorname{NegGrad}$ | 0.980 | 0.975 | 0.953 | 0.628 |
| $\operatorname{Fisher}$ | 0.973 | 0.967 | 0.942 | 0.709 |
| $\operatorname{SalUN}$ | 0.979 | 0.972 | 0.945 | 0.689 |
| $\operatorname{SSD}$ | **0.996** | **0.988** | **0.981** | 0.888 |
| $\operatorname{Retrain}$ | 0.998 | 0.996 | 0.997 | **0.993** |

</div>

Every row is monotonically decreasing in $\epsilon$ — exactly the *negative correlation* the certified-removal lower bound demands. $\mathcal{Q}(\operatorname{Retrain}) \approx 1$ at every $\epsilon$, empirically reaffirming zero grounding.

The two prior metrics fail one or both tests. **MIA score** ($1 - \text{MIA AUC}$): $\operatorname{NegGrad}$ jumps from 0.476 ($\epsilon = 50$) to 0.482 ($\epsilon = 150$), then *drops* to 0.466 ($\epsilon = 600$) — not monotone. **IC score**: $\operatorname{NegGrad}$ goes 0.925 → 0.953 → 0.946 across the same $\epsilon$ sequence, and the relative ranking of $\operatorname{None}$ versus $\operatorname{NegGrad}$ flips between $\epsilon = 50$ and $\epsilon = \infty$. Only $\mathcal{Q}$ satisfies both the negative-correlation test and the consistency test.

$\mathcal{Q}$ is, to our knowledge, the first empirical unlearning metric that is simultaneously theory-grounded *and* practical to compute.

<div class="takeaway">

**Takeaway.** Treating the MIA as the *adversary* in a two-player game — rather than as the metric itself — gives unlearning evaluation what it lacked: a worst-case guarantee, an automatic zero-grounding of retraining ($\mathcal{Q}(\operatorname{Retrain}) = 1$), and a formal $(\epsilon, \delta)$-certified-removal lower bound. The SWAP test makes the worst-case computable in two splits, and the metric tightens *automatically* as stronger MIAs come along.

</div>
